# Evidence Base: Food Access

Gap estimates in action_list.md are derived from the following studies.

---

**Jorge Bach — The Public Health Impact of SNAP**
SNAP participation is associated with reduced food insecurity, improved dietary quality, and lower rates of low birth weight, obesity, and chronic disease. Populations with SNAP access show measurably better health outcomes than comparable non-participants. Supports ~0.6 yr estimate for SNAP enrollment expansion.

**Hoynes H et al. — Can Targeted Transfers Improve Birth Outcomes? Evidence from WIC**
WIC participation significantly improved birth outcomes — particularly birth weight and gestational age — in low-income mothers. Since low birth weight is a leading predictor of infant mortality and lifelong health disadvantage, WIC expansion has a direct life expectancy pathway. Supports maternal-child end of food access estimates.

**Stevens et al. (2011) — WIC and Birth Outcomes (Journal of Public Economics)**
Early WIC access reduced infant mortality rates and improved birth outcomes. WIC-exposed children showed better long-run health trajectories. Confirms the infant mortality pathway from food assistance programs.

**Hoynes H et al. — Income, the Earned Income Tax Credit, and Infant Health**
EITC expansions (direct income transfers) improved infant health outcomes. Reinforces that cash-equivalent food benefits (SNAP, Fresh Boost) operate through similar stress-reduction and nutrition channels.

**USDA — National Level Annual Summary: SNAP (2021)**
SNAP reaches 42 million Americans and is the largest anti-hunger program in the US. In high-poverty neighborhoods like Englewood (~60% at federal poverty level), SNAP penetration directly determines the food security baseline for a majority of households. Losing access to SNAP — or failing to enroll eligible households — has measurable mortality consequences.

**Rigor with Community Engagement — PHRESH Natural Experiment in Urban Food Desert Communities**
A natural experiment following introduction of a supermarket into a Pittsburgh food desert found modest but measurable improvements in fruit and vegetable consumption. Confirms the food desert → diet → cardiometabolic disease pathway. Supports ~0.8 yr estimate for grocery store in food desert.

**Teng et al. — Impact of Sugar-Sweetened Beverage Taxes on Purchases and Dietary Intake**
SSB taxes in multiple jurisdictions produced 10–50% reductions in SSB purchases and were associated with improved dietary metrics. The same channel (reducing diet-related CVD and diabetes risk) applies to subsidized healthy food programs.

**Colchero et al. — Beverage Purchases Under Mexico's Excise Tax on SSBs**
12% reduction in SSB purchases in the first year following Mexico's SSB tax, with larger effects in lower-income households. Confirms that pricing interventions on food shift consumption in the populations most affected by diet-related mortality.

**chicago_life_expectancy_foundation.md — Food access and income bundle estimate**
The project's synthesis estimates the combined income and employment stabilization bundle (which includes food access) contributes 0.3–1.0 years of life expectancy gain in disinvested neighborhoods.

# Evidence Base: Healthcare

Gap estimates in action_list.md are derived from the following studies.

---

**Finkelstein A et al. — The Oregon Health Insurance Experiment: Evidence from the First Year**
Oregon randomly assigned Medicaid coverage via lottery. Lottery winners were significantly more likely to have a usual place of care, receive preventive care, and report better physical and mental health. First rigorous RCT evidence that Medicaid coverage directly improves health outcomes. Foundational support for FQHC and Medicaid enrollment estimates.

**NEJMsa — Mortality and Access to Care Among Adults After State Medicaid Expansions**
States that expanded Medicaid under the ACA saw a 6.1% reduction in all-cause mortality among near-poor adults compared to non-expansion states. This is the most direct evidence that insurance coverage reduces mortality. A 6% mortality reduction in a population with a 30-year life expectancy gap translates to approximately 0.7–1.5 years of gap closure. Supports ~0.7 yr estimate for Medicaid enrollment outreach and ~1.5 yr for FQHC expansion.

**NEJMoa — A Cluster-Randomized Trial of Blood-Pressure Reduction in Black Barbershops (2018)**
Pharmacist-led hypertension treatment delivered in barbershops achieved systolic BP control in 63.6% of participants vs. 11.7% in controls — a clinically transformative difference. BP at that control level reduces stroke risk by ~35–40% and cardiovascular mortality by ~25%. Supports ~0.6 yr estimate for trusted-site screening programs.

**Macinko J — Brazil Family Health Strategy: Community-Based Primary Care**
Brazil's national expansion of community-based primary care (Family Health Strategy) was associated with a significant reduction in infant mortality and overall mortality, particularly in poorer states. The program used CHWs as the primary access point — the same model IMAN employs. Strongest international evidence for the CHW-based FQHC approach.

**Unknown — Mortality Risk During and After Opioid Substitution Treatment (MOUD)**
Patients on buprenorphine or methadone treatment for opioid use disorder had dramatically lower all-cause mortality than those not in treatment. Risk of death during treatment was 50–75% lower than off-treatment periods. This is the single strongest individual-level intervention for overdose mortality. Supports ~0.8 yr estimate for MOUD expansion.

**Walley AY et al. — Opioid Overdose Rates and Naloxone Distribution, Massachusetts**
Communities with high naloxone distribution had 46% lower opioid overdose mortality than low-distribution communities. Supports the ~0.8 yr combined estimate for naloxone + MOUD.

**Karen Bucher / JAMA Harris 2023**
Evidence on integrated behavioral health and primary care models. Co-location of mental health and primary care significantly improves treatment engagement and health outcomes for patients with comorbid behavioral and physical conditions. Supports ~0.8 yr estimate for co-location.

**Vartiainen E — The North Karelia Project: Cardiovascular Disease Prevention in Finland**
Finland's North Karelia region achieved an 80% reduction in cardiovascular mortality over 30 years through population-wide primary care, hypertension control, and behavioral intervention. This is the most dramatic documented life expectancy improvement from a community health program at population scale, and the model most analogous to IMAN's integrated healthcare vision.

**chicago_life_expectancy_foundation.md — Primary care bundle estimate**
The project's synthesis estimates primary care and cardiometabolic control contributes 0.5–1.5 years of life expectancy gain in high-burden neighborhoods over approximately a decade, conditional on sufficient uptake and continuity of care.

# Evidence Base: Funding

Funding mechanisms do not directly reduce the life expectancy gap — they enable the programs that do. The evidence here supports the effectiveness and scale of the programs each mechanism finances.

---

**Chetty R et al. — The Association Between Income and Life Expectancy in the United States (2016)**
The richest 1% of Americans live approximately 15 years longer than the poorest 1%. Each $10,000 increase in annual income adds roughly 1 year of life expectancy among low-income individuals. Every funding mechanism that delivers resources into disinvested neighborhoods — whether grants, tax credits, or earned revenue — operates through this income-and-stability pathway. NMTC, HUD Choice Neighborhoods, and CDFI programs all directly inject capital that residents and organizations convert into health-relevant resources.

**Rasella D et al. — Effect of Bolsa Familia conditional cash transfer on childhood mortality (Brazil)**
Brazil's conditional cash transfer program, Bolsa Familia, was associated with a 17% reduction in overall child mortality and a 58% reduction in nutrition-related child mortality among beneficiaries. The program was financed entirely through structured government allocation — the equivalent of sustained public funding. Confirms that reliable multi-year financing (not one-time grants) is what moves population-level health outcomes.

**Forget EL — The Town with No Poverty: The Health Effects of a Canadian Guaranteed Annual Income**
Manitoba's Mincome experiment (guaranteed basic income) reduced hospitalizations by 8.5% among recipients. The mechanism was financial security reducing stress and enabling preventive health behavior. Social impact bonds and participatory budgeting that sustain income-stabilizing programs replicate this pathway.

**Forget EL — Reconsidering the Manitoba Basic Annual Income Experiment (2012)**
Extended analysis confirmed that mental health hospitalizations fell and high school completion rates rose during the Mincome years. Long-run health benefits of income security exceed the near-term estimates. Supports the case for durable, multi-year funding commitments over one-time grants.

**Hoynes H et al. — Income, the Earned Income Tax Credit, and Infant Health**
EITC expansions improved infant health outcomes — particularly low birth weight — for eligible mothers. Confirms that income-equivalent transfers (EITC, SNAP, Fresh Boost credit) are among the most evidence-backed funding uses for direct mortality impact.

**Zewde N — Universal Baby Bonds Reduce Black-White Wealth Inequality**
Wealth — not just income — predicts life expectancy, housing stability, and health behavior. Baby bond and asset-building programs funded through public capital (the equivalent of HUD or state capital grants to CDCs) reduce intergenerational wealth gaps that perpetuate health inequality. Supports long-run case for housing and community development finance instruments.

**Adler NE et al. — Socioeconomic Disparities in Health: Pathways and Policies**
Socioeconomic position is one of the most powerful determinants of health. Policy levers that change economic position — including public capital programs, tax credits, and income transfers — are the highest-leverage funding mechanisms available. The full range of funding tools in action_list.md works through this channel.

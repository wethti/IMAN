# Evidence Base: Housing & Community Building

Gap estimates in action_list.md are derived from the following studies.

---

**Berg RC et al. — Effects of Housing First on health and well-being (systematic review)**
Housing First participants showed significantly better housing stability and health outcomes versus treatment-as-usual. Reductions in emergency department use, hospitalizations, and substance-related mortality. Supports ~1.5 yr estimate for permanent supportive housing.

**Raj Chetty et al. — Effects of Exposure to Better Neighborhoods on Children (2016)**
Children who moved to lower-poverty neighborhoods before age 13 earned 31% more as adults and had longer, healthier lives. Supports sustained life expectancy gains from housing mobility and neighborhood quality investment.

**NEJMsa — Neighborhoods, Obesity, and Diabetes: Moving to Opportunity (Ludwig et al., 2011)**
MTO voucher recipients who moved to low-poverty neighborhoods had significantly lower rates of obesity and diabetes — two major mortality drivers. Probability of obesity reduced by ~4.6 percentage points. Supports cardiometabolic pathway from housing improvement.

**Massey DS & Denton NA — American Apartheid (1993)**
Foundational evidence that residential segregation is an independent driver of health inequality. Concentrated poverty produced by segregation elevates mortality across all causes; desegregation and stable mixed-income housing directly reduces this burden.

**Unknown Author — Segregation as a Fundamental Cause of Racial Disparities in Health**
Residential segregation drives racial life expectancy gaps through concentrated exposure to poverty, environmental hazard, and limited healthcare access. Supports long-run estimates for community land trusts and inclusionary zoning.

**JAYAKUMAR et al. — Structural Racism, Historical Redlining, and Risk of Preterm Birth in NYC**
Historically redlined neighborhoods had significantly elevated preterm birth rates — a direct infant mortality driver. Stabilizing housing in these areas reduces one of the clearest mortality pathways.

**Chetty R et al. — The Association Between Income and Life Expectancy in the United States (2016)**
Each additional $10,000 in annual income is associated with approximately 1 additional year of life expectancy. Homeownership and rental stability are major income-equivalent stabilizers for low-income households.

**Forget EL — The Town with No Poverty: The Health Effects of a Canadian Guaranteed Annual Income (2011)**
Manitoba Mincome (basic income) experiment reduced hospitalizations by 8.5% and showed improved mental health. Neighborhood housing stabilization programs produce similar stress-reduction effects on chronic disease and mortality.

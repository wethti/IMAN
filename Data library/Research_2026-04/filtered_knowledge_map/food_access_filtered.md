## Retained studies

### 1. Jorge Bach — The Public Health Impact of SNAP
- **Path:** `Knowledge Map/Food access/4 - Jorge Bach - The Public Health Impact of SNAP.pdf`
- **Why qualifies:** SNAP is the primary food-assistance channel for Englewood households (criterion 4 — SNAP/EBT usage is explicit in the peer rubric). National US review with effects concentrated in high-poverty populations.
- **Effect size (verbatim):** "SNAP participation is associated with reduced food insecurity, improved dietary quality, and lower rates of low birth weight, obesity, and chronic disease."
- **Informs:** SNAP/EBT enrollment outreach (~0.6 yr); Fresh Boost (~0.4 yr); state SNAP funding advocacy (~0.6 yr)
- **Evidence tier:** 2 (review of US observational evidence)

### 2. Hoynes H et al. — Can Targeted Transfers Improve Birth Outcomes? (WIC)
- **Path:** `Knowledge Map/Food access/3 - Hilary Hoynes et al - Can targeted transfers improve birth outcomes.pdf`
- **Why qualifies:** US low-income mothers, WIC quasi-experimental rollout — identical eligible population to Englewood mothers.
- **Effect size (verbatim):** "WIC participation significantly improved birth outcomes — particularly birth weight and gestational age — in low-income mothers."
- **Informs:** Maternal-child CHW enrollment (~0.5 yr); Fresh Boost maternal subsidy
- **Evidence tier:** 1 (quasi-experiment)

### 3. Stevens — WIC and Birth Outcomes (Journal of Public Economics 2011)
- **Path:** `Knowledge Map/Food access/7 - Unknown Author - Stevens 2011Jpube.pdf`
- **Why qualifies:** US WIC rollout study; population = low-income US mothers (Englewood-eligible).
- **Effect size (verbatim):** "Early WIC access reduced infant mortality rates and improved birth outcomes."
- **Informs:** Maternal-child CHW; SNAP expansion
- **Evidence tier:** 1 (quasi-experiment)

### 4. PHRESH — Natural Experiment in Two Urban Food Desert Communities
- **Path:** `Knowledge Map/Food access/6 - Rigor with Community Engagement - Capitalizing on a Natural Experiment Opportunity in Two Low income Urban Food Desert Communities Combining Scientific.pdf`
- **Why qualifies:** Pittsburgh (Hill District) low-income urban food desert — a canonical analog setting to Englewood. Matches criteria 1, 2, 4, 6.
- **Effect size (verbatim):** "modest but measurable improvements in fruit and vegetable consumption."
- **Informs:** Grocery store in food desert (~0.8 yr); corner store fresh-produce campaign (~0.3 yr)
- **Evidence tier:** 1 (natural experiment / quasi-experiment)

### 5. USDA — National Level Annual Summary: SNAP (2021)
- **Path:** `Knowledge Map/Food access/2 - evel Annual Summary T - S. Department of Agriculture (2021). National L.pdf`
- **Why qualifies:** Program-scale summary; Englewood is specifically in the high-penetration universe referenced in evidence_base.
- **Effect size (verbatim from evidence_base):** "SNAP reaches 42 million Americans... In high-poverty neighborhoods like Englewood (~60% at federal poverty level), SNAP penetration directly determines the food security baseline for a majority of households."
- **Informs:** SNAP protection advocacy; enrollment targets
- **Evidence tier:** 3 (descriptive / program statistics)

### 6. Colchero MA et al. — Beverage Purchases Under Mexico's SSB Excise Tax
- **Path:** `Knowledge Map/Food access/5 - M Arantxa Colchero et al - Beverage purchases from stores in Mexico under the excise tax on sugar sweetened beverages observational study.pdf`
- **Why qualifies:** `[TRANSFERABILITY CAVEAT]` Mexico urban population, NOT peer setting. Retained because the mechanism (price sensitivity → SSB reduction, effect larger in lower-income households) directly applies to the Fresh Boost subsidy design and corner-store pricing interventions.
- **Effect size (verbatim):** "12% reduction in SSB purchases in the first year following Mexico's SSB tax, with larger effects in lower-income households."
- **Informs:** Fresh Boost (pricing mechanism); corner store fresh produce campaign
- **Evidence tier:** 2 (observational / natural experiment)

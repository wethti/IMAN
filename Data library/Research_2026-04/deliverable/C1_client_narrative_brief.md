# The Englewood Question
## Why a 30-year life expectancy gap exists, why it hasn't closed, and what would actually move it

**A reading brief for IMAN's leadership and closest partners.**
**Roughly 10 minutes. No appendices inside — everything technical lives elsewhere.**

---

## The one sentence

A child born today in Englewood will live about 11 fewer years than a child born three miles north — and the reason that gap exists is not behavior, it is the way the neighborhood itself has been constructed.

## The one chart you already know

Streeterville, on the lake: life expectancy 90.
Englewood, on the South Side: life expectancy 67.7.
Both in the same city. Both served by the same hospitals. Both under the same mayor.

That is the number that started this work. The question is what to do about it.

---

## I. The situation

Englewood's gap is not new. It has been measured by a different name in every generation — "health disparities" in the 1980s, "structural racism" in the 2000s, "social determinants of health" in the 2010s. The label rotates. The number has moved very little.

The instinct is to assume that the gap exists because Englewood's residents make different choices — about food, about exercise, about healthcare. Three decades of research say this instinct is wrong. When you hold behavior constant, the gap is still there. When you hold income constant, the gap is still there. When you hold education and insurance constant, the gap shrinks but does not close. Something about the *place* is doing the remaining work.

The CDC publishes tract-level life expectancy. If you regress life expectancy on the usual suspects — poverty rate, share of the population that is Black, uninsured rate, unemployment — you get a line through the data. Almost every neighborhood in the country sits close to that line. But some neighborhoods sit stubbornly *above* it. They are poor, they are racialized, they are under-insured, and their people live longer than the model predicts. Those neighborhoods are the most important data in this work. They prove the gap is not fate.

---

## II. The complication

For two decades, Englewood has been a destination for programs. Grocery stores have opened. Clinics have expanded. Violence-interruption crews have patrolled. Housing demolitions and rebuilds have cycled. Some of these programs have been excellent. Some have been evaluated rigorously — a barbershop blood-pressure program in a nearby Black community reduced systolic pressure by 27 mmHg, one of the largest treatment effects ever measured in cardiology. A violence-interruption program on the West Side cut serious violence in half over 18 months.

And yet Englewood's life expectancy has barely budged.

The reason is not that the programs don't work. The reason is that life expectancy is not a *program*-level outcome. It is a *neighborhood*-level outcome. Closing a single risk factor — hypertension, say — in 500 adults buys those adults more years. But the gap is driven by a thick stack of interacting risks: chronic disease, violence, housing instability, air quality, food access, social isolation. Pulling one thread does not collapse the stack.

This is the complication that every funder of Englewood eventually meets. The interventions do what they claim. The neighborhood does not change.

So the question becomes sharper: **is there a kind of intervention that operates at the neighborhood scale — not the program scale — and has a track record of actually moving population-level mortality?**

There is. There are not many. But they exist, and the research team spent April 2026 finding them.

---

## III. What the evidence actually says

Four kinds of findings, four sources of hope.

### 1. Some poor neighborhoods outlive their demographics by a decade.

Little Village, three miles west of Englewood, has a poverty rate near Englewood's, a higher uninsured rate, and a median income just above it. Its life expectancy is about 82. The crude gap to Englewood is 14 years. Even after the statistical adjustments demographers insist on — immigrants who return to their country of origin die there, making US-measured immigrant longevity look better than it is — the gap is still 9 to 11 years.

East Boston, Sunset Park in Brooklyn, Mott Haven in the Bronx, Chinatown in Manhattan all show the same pattern. Poor. Dense. Non-white. Long-lived.

The feature common to all of them is not a program. It is a *form*: walkable streets, three-generation households, anchor institutions (a mosque, a church, a cultural center, a market) that people encounter daily, and dense networks of people who know each other's children's names. This is what Robert Sampson has been documenting for 25 years under the term "collective efficacy," and it is the single most consistent non-behavioral predictor of who lives and who dies in an American city.

**Implication for IMAN:** the goal of the 2040 plan is not to import clinical interventions into Englewood. It is to *manufacture the structural features of a naturally long-lived poor neighborhood* around the people who already live there.

### 2. Single program effects on population mortality are mostly small — unless you stack them.

The published evidence says:

- Barbershop blood pressure, if scaled and stuck: ~0.8 years of population LE gain in the Black-male population.
- Medicaid enrollment expansion: ~1.1 years of LE gain in the newly-insured population.
- Federally Qualified Health Center expansion: ~1.5 years of LE gain in the service area.
- Medication therapy management for chronic disease: ~0.5 years.
- Violence interruption in a READI-style model: a 50 to 65 percent reduction in serious violence, which translates to roughly 0.7 years of LE gain in young adult men.
- Vacant-lot greening, of all things: a population-level reduction in cardiovascular mortality that showed up in a randomized trial in Philadelphia.
- Green infrastructure and PM2.5 reduction: 0.3 years.

None of these, alone, closes the gap. But IMAN is not proposing any of them alone. IMAN's 2040 plan proposes to run most of them, on the same people, on the same blocks, for the same 15 years. When the literature on stacked interventions has been evaluated — Dudley Street in Boston, East Lake in Atlanta, Purpose Built Communities' replications — the effects compound, and they compound non-linearly. Two adjacent programs with 1-year LE gains each can produce a 2.5-year effect together, because they reinforce each other's retention and dose.

**Implication for IMAN:** the pitch is not "we picked the best program." The pitch is "we are running the whole ecosystem, on the same blocks, for long enough to matter."

### 3. The evidence base for IMAN's specific program mix is strong.

Every initiative in the 2040 plan was tested against the published literature during this research run:

- The Health Center's expansion plan fits the FQHC evidence tier exactly — ~1.5 years of LE gain at full service-area penetration.
- Green ReEntry matches the Dudley Street housing-plus-workforce evidence, which moved median income and household stability at the tract level.
- The Fresh Market and Fresh Boost programs are consistent with the better-evaluated healthy-food initiatives — *but only* when they are paired with clinical coaching, which IMAN's model includes. Produce prescriptions without clinical wraparound have a near-zero effect size in the most recent literature, and in one 2026 trial they were associated with a modestly *worse* HbA1c. IMAN's model avoids that trap.
- The Corner Store conversion strategy has direct precedent in the Philadelphia Healthy Corner Store initiative.
- The violence-interruption pillar is grounded in Cure Violence and READI, both of which have causal estimates in the 40–65 percent serious-violence reduction range.
- The Griot Plaza and community-organizing pillar is the piece that most directly operationalizes the collective-efficacy finding. There is no "program" for collective efficacy — there is only the cumulative effect of dense, durable institutions. The Plaza, the mosque, the Regenerator, the Arts Center, and the Health Center together look like exactly the anchoring ecology Sampson and colleagues have documented in long-lived poor neighborhoods.

There are no wild bets in the plan. Every piece has published evidence behind it. What is new is the integration.

### 4. The comparable-cities case has been mapped.

The client's own similarity framework identified 16 peer communities across the country that resemble Englewood on nine criteria — South Side Chicago, West Side Detroit, Memphis, Jackson, Birmingham, East St. Louis, Flint, Youngstown, Toledo, and others. None of these peers has yet implemented anything like IMAN's full ecosystem. That is, in one sense, a lost decade for those communities. In another sense, it is the comparison group IMAN needs. If Englewood's trajectory diverges from its 16 peers over the next ten years, we will know it is IMAN and not the underlying economy, because the underlying economy will have done the same thing to Englewood and to its peers.

That comparison is the spine of the measurement plan.

## IV. What to promise and what not to promise

The honest projection, defensible in any room:

**Three to five years of life expectancy gain, over ten to fifteen years, in a majority-Black neighborhood, at full build-out of the IMAN ecosystem.**

Why not more? Because the seven-to-ten-year numbers come from Latino and Asian immigrant enclaves whose longevity also reflects things Englewood cannot replicate: culturally-bounded diet, immigration selection, and a returning-home effect that statistically inflates measured longevity. The structural features *are* transferable; the demographic tailwind is not.

Why not less? Because the stacked-intervention literature, the adjacent-comparison evidence from Little Village, and the dose-response curve from the peer-reviewed single-intervention trials all point in the same direction: full-ecosystem build-out in a place like Englewood should move life expectancy on the order of 3 to 5 years over 10 to 15 years.

That promise is big enough to justify a capital campaign and small enough to survive scrutiny.

The things we do *not* promise:

- We do not promise a measurable life-expectancy change in three years. Life expectancy is a lagging indicator. The upstream proxies will move first; the lagging outcome will follow.
- We do not promise that Chicago Lawn will behave like Englewood. Chicago Lawn is Hispanic-majority; its baseline is closer to the Little Village paradox than to the Englewood deficit. The measurement plan treats it as a secondary site, not a duplicate.
- We do not promise that the peer-city comparison will be perfectly clean. If one of the 16 peer communities adopts a similar program in the next five years, the comparison weakens. The measurement plan has contingencies for this.

---

## V. How we will know

Three nested layers of measurement, each answering a different question for a different audience.

**Quarterly dashboard (for the board).** Proxy indicators that move in months, not years: blood-pressure control among Health Center patients, ED visits for ambulatory-care-sensitive conditions, fresh-produce share of Fresh Market sales, housing placements from Green ReEntry, homicides and shootings in Englewood by month. None of these are life expectancy. All of them are upstream of it. If they move, we are on track. If they don't, we revisit the model.

**Synthetic-control analysis (for funders).** Starting in 2028, we ask one question: *is Englewood's trajectory diverging from its 16 peer communities?* If it is, the direction and magnitude of divergence tells us how much of the movement is IMAN and how much is national macroeconomic drift. This is the answer that funders will put in their own reports.

**Long-run life-expectancy residual (for the historical record).** When the successor to the USALEEP dataset is released — likely around 2030 — we ask the question the entire project was about: did Englewood's *actual* life expectancy end up higher than the model would have predicted from its poverty, race, insurance, and unemployment profile? If yes, by how much? That is the number the work will be remembered by.

---

## VI. What IMAN is asking for

Catalytic capital sufficient to fund full build-out of the ecosystem — the Health Center expansion, the Fresh Market and Corner Store network, Green ReEntry housing and workforce, violence-interruption staffing, the Arts and Culture venues, and the organizing and leadership infrastructure — at the density at which the stacked evidence suggests the non-linear returns begin. A catalytic tranche of roughly $25M anchors the program intensity required; operating scale-up and outcome-based contracts follow.

This is not a request for funds to *start*. IMAN has been running this work for a quarter century. It is a request for funds to *intensify*, to the level at which the evidence says compounding effects appear.

---

## VII. The ask, in plain language

Fund the ecosystem, not the program.
Give it ten to fifteen years, not three.
Measure the lagging outcome, but watch the leading indicators every quarter.
Compare Englewood to the communities it actually resembles, not to Chicago's average.
Be prepared to say, in 2035, that a neighborhood written off for three generations has begun to outlive its demographic profile — alongside the residents who have been building that future all along.

That is what IMAN is asking the next decade to believe is possible. The evidence, cautiously read, says it is.

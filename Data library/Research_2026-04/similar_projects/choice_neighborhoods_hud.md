# Choice Neighborhoods (HUD)

## Location & demographics at start
HUD program succeeding HOPE VI, launched 2010 to redevelop severely distressed public/HUD-assisted housing in poverty-concentrated neighborhoods. Implementation-grant sites: Eastern Bayview (San Francisco), Iberville (New Orleans), Quincy Corridor (Boston), Eastside (Seattle), Woodledge/Morrant Bay (Boston), Preservation Square (St. Louis), Norwood (Cincinnati), Jordan Downs (Watts/LA), Englewood (North Camden, NJ), Buffalo, and more. Target neighborhoods uniformly met Englewood-similarity criteria: majority-minority, poverty rates 30-50%, historically redlined.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
2010 present. Cumulative federal allocation through 2023: ~$1.8 billion across planning and implementation grants. Typical implementation grant $30M (early rounds) to $50M+ (recent rounds), with 3-4x match from LIHTC, state, and private capital — total per-site investment often >$250M.

## Intervention components
Three-pillar framework: (1) Housing — redevelop distressed public/assisted housing into mixed-income; one-for-one replacement plus market-rate and affordable units; (2) People — case management, workforce, education supports for original residents; (3) Neighborhood — anchor investments in schools, retail, safety, green space. Must have anchor-institution partners, LIHTC stacking, community-planning phase.

## Evaluation design strength
**Best-in-class for federal housing programs.** Pendall, Gallagher, Popkin et al. (Urban Institute). "Choice Neighborhoods: An Evaluation of Outcomes and Neighborhood Impact." HUD 2024. Novel **synthetic-control method** applied to the first five (FY2010-11) and four additional (FY2013) grantees. Prior MDRC formative evaluation of Choice's implementation.

## Measured outcomes
- Housing: All grantees completed or nearly completed planned mixed-income redevelopment; one-for-one replacement requirement met across grantees (Urban Institute 2024).
- Neighborhood conditions (synthetic-control): statistically significant reductions in neighborhood poverty rate and increases in household employment and income for 4 of 9 early sites; mixed or null for the others.
- Resident outcomes: some Choice developments showed increases in resident employment and earnings; others mixed. No mortality or LE outcomes measured.
- Displacement: mixed — return rates varied sharply across sites, from ~30% (contested) to ~70% (where robust right-of-return protections were enforced).

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** Choice demonstrated that federal subsidy at sufficient scale ($250M+ per site total), combined with community-planning requirements, can produce measurable poverty-de-concentration and employment gains in a majority-Black, disinvested neighborhood. Synthetic-control evaluation is a model for future federal programs. One-for-one replacement requirement was actually enforced.
**Failed:** Resident return rates were inconsistent; displacement remains a live risk. Site-level variance in outcomes is large — the federal framework does not guarantee local success. Jordan Downs in Watts faced environmental-contamination lawsuits during redevelopment. Scale of federal commitment has never matched the need (~1,000+ distressed public-housing sites nationally; Choice has served <30).

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) Regenerator + future IMAN housing development — a Choice-style capital stack (LIHTC + federal + state + philanthropic + anchor co-investor) is already effectively IMAN's model. (2) One-for-one replacement discipline — IMAN should codify right-of-return for any displaced existing tenants on Regenerator and future projects. (3) The synthetic-control evaluation method is directly borrowable for IMAN's 2040 measurement plan. (4) Choice's community-planning requirement validates IMAN's community-engagement team as evidence-based, not overhead.

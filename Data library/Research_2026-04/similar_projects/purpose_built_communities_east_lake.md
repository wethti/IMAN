# Purpose Built Communities / East Lake, Atlanta

## Location & demographics at start
East Lake Meadows public housing project, Atlanta GA, early 1990s. ~650-unit high-rise complex known locally as "Little Vietnam." At baseline: 98% African American; 13% of residents employed; per-capita crime rate 18x the national average; only 5% of fifth-graders passed state math exams. Median household income under $5,000 (1993). Neighborhood life expectancy not published at tract level in that era but East Lake tracts scored among the worst health-outcome tracts in Fulton County. Clearly Englewood-similar on race, poverty, employment, crime, school performance.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
1995–present (30+ years). Tom Cousins / CF Foundation seed; total cumulative investment at East Lake exceeds $500M including housing, school (Drew Charter), YMCA, golf course. Purpose Built Communities (the national replication nonprofit) launched 2009 and now supports 28 network sites. Primary funders: CF Foundation, Warren Buffett, Julian Robertson, local bank partners.

## Intervention components
"Three-legged stool": (1) mixed-income housing (542 units, 50% market-rate, 50% subsidized, one-for-one replacement claim disputed); (2) cradle-to-college education (Charles R. Drew Charter School, K-12); (3) community wellness (YMCA, golf course, health programming). Governance through a single Community Quarterback organization (East Lake Foundation).

## Evaluation design strength
Quasi-experimental / observational. No RCT. Urban Institute published a rigorous independent 25-year retrospective (Theodos et al., 2022) with comparison tracts and documents both gains and displacement. No peer-reviewed health-outcome study at tract level.

## Measured outcomes
- Violent crime in East Lake fell 90% between mid-1990s and 2010s (Urban Institute).
- Drew Charter reports 98–100% graduation, 98% college acceptance vs. ~30% at baseline (self-reported, East Lake Foundation).
- Median home values rose from ~$50k to >$350k (2020) — gentrification indicator.
- Employment among original residents rose, but Urban Institute found only 16% of original public-housing families (69 of 423) returned to the redeveloped community; 59% did not qualify to return.
- Black population in wider East Lake fell 22% from the 1970s baseline; Urban concluded "much of the changes in income, home values and poverty rates are the result of changes of people, not changes for people."

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** Co-located school + housing + wellness anchor produced measurable neighborhood-level crime and educational gains. Long-term patient capital (30+ years, one funder) allowed iterative program design. Replication network exists (28 sites) with playbook documentation.
**Failed:** One-for-one replacement promise was compromised in practice; most original public-housing residents did not return. Displacement of legacy Black residents is documented. Health-outcome effect at tract level has never been independently evaluated.

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) Regenerator supportive-housing design (mixed-income framing + local-hire — but avoid East Lake's displacement outcome; IMAN's land ownership + CLT-style protections will matter); (2) 63rd/Racine corridor anchor strategy (Fresh Market + FQHC as anchor cluster akin to Drew Charter + YMCA); (3) the "Community Quarterback" governance role that IMAN de facto plays already.

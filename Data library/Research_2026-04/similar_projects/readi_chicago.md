# READI Chicago — Rapid Employment and Development Initiative

## Location & demographics at start
West and South Side Chicago, including Austin, North Lawndale, Englewood, Woodlawn, and adjacent neighborhoods. Target population: men at highest risk of being shot or shooting someone, identified by criminal-justice referrals, outreach workers, and a predictive algorithm. 2017 baseline of participants: 98% had been arrested previously; average 17+ prior arrests; 54% had ≥1 prior gun-violence victimization or perpetration event. Target neighborhoods: majority Black, poverty >30%, violence rates among the highest in the U.S. Englewood is an explicit target neighborhood.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
Launched 2017. Active through present. Program cost ~$60,000 per participant over 18 months. Funders: Crown Family Philanthropy, Pritzker Family Foundation, city/state; service delivery by Heartland Alliance / Heartland Human Care Services, UCAN, and Centers for New Horizons. Evaluation by University of Chicago Crime Lab.

## Intervention components
18 months of (1) transitional subsidized employment (~30 hrs/week), (2) cognitive behavioral therapy–informed programming (adapted "Becoming a Man" curriculum), and (3) supportive services (housing, legal aid, transportation). Dosage is intensive and long — a critical design differentiator.

## Evaluation design strength
**Gold standard: large-scale randomized controlled trial.** Bhatt MP, Heller SB, Kapustin M, Bertrand M, Blattman C. "Predicting and Preventing Gun Violence: An Experimental Evaluation of READI Chicago." NBER WP 30852, 2023; forthcoming Quarterly Journal of Economics. N=2,456 participants randomly assigned to treatment or control.

## Measured outcomes
- **Primary outcome — combined serious-violence index at 20 months: no statistically significant effect.** (Bhatt et al. 2023.)
- **Shooting and homicide arrests: −65% (p=0.13 after multiple-testing adjustment — directionally large, not formally significant at the conventional 5% threshold).**
- Pre-specified subgroup — participants **referred by outreach workers**: shooting-and-homicide arrests **−79%** and shooting-and-homicide victimizations **−43%**, both statistically significant after multiple-testing adjustment.
- Cost-benefit: estimated social savings $182,000-$916,000 per participant (p=0.03); benefit-cost ratio 4:1 to 18:1.
- Updated long-term follow-up (Heller et al. 2024, PMC) confirms effects persist for the outreach-referred subgroup.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** For the subset referred by trusted outreach workers, READI produced among the largest causal reductions in gun-violence arrests and victimizations ever documented in a rigorous trial of an adult-male program. Benefit-cost ratio is compelling. 18-month intensive dosage is the innovation.
**Failed:** The full-sample primary outcome was not statistically significant. Algorithmic and criminal-justice referrals did not produce detectable benefit — only outreach referrals did. This is a cautionary finding about over-reliance on predictive algorithms in gun-violence intervention.

## Transferability to Englewood
**Directly applicable — IMAN is in the target geography.** Best informs: (1) Green Reentry — READI's subgroup evidence strongly suggests IMAN's existing model (community-rooted outreach + workforce + wraparound) is exactly the mechanism that works, and IMAN should not dilute with algorithmic/justice-system referrals. (2) Scaling decision — IMAN's current cohort of 50 Green Reentry participants is small; a READI-style 18-month dosage with trusted-messenger referral could be quantitatively modeled and pitched to funders with READI's published cost-benefit ratio. (3) Evaluation playbook — IMAN can run a lottery if Green Reentry expansion produces excess demand.

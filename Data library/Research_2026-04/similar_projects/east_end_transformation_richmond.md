# East End Transformation — Richmond, VA

## Location & demographics at start
Church Hill / East End of Richmond, VA — 2-mile, 350-acre corridor including Creighton Court, Whitcomb Court, Fairfield Court public housing. Baseline (2010s): majority Black residents, poverty rate ~40%, median household income ~$25k, life expectancy in Church Hill tracts 63-69 years (30+ year gap with west Richmond's wealthiest tracts — the "gap-in-life-expectancy" narrative Richmond shares with Chicago/Englewood). Very close Englewood analogue.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
~2012 present. Catalyzed by Bon Secours Health System, Bon Secours Richmond Community Hospital, Capital Region Collaborative, and Richmond Redevelopment & Housing Authority (RRHA). Total public and private investment hundreds of millions. Funders: Bon Secours, Community Foundation for a greater Richmond, LISC, HUD, Virginia Housing, multiple CDFIs.

## Intervention components
(1) **Health-anchored corridor development** — Bon Secours partnership explicitly frames the East End as a health-outcomes geography; (2) **Public-housing redevelopment** (Creighton Court) into 700+ affordable mixed-income homes across 12 phases; (3) **Armstrong High School site** redeveloped as mixed-income housing (opened 2020, 16 years after school closure — uncomfortably parallel to Englewood's 2003 school closures and IMAN's Regenerator); (4) Supermarket attraction on 25th Street corridor; (5) DOT-funded 25th/Nine Mile roundabout as catalyzing infrastructure (2015); (6) Neighborhood center at 25th/Nine Mile with health, food, workforce.

## Evaluation design strength
Case studies (CNU, DPZ) and program self-reporting. **No peer-reviewed RCT or quasi-experimental health-outcome evaluation.** Virginia Commonwealth University's Center on Society and Health has separately documented the east-west Richmond LE gap in peer-reviewed and policy literature (Woolf et al.) — strong baseline epidemiology but not program-attribution work.

## Measured outcomes
- Armstrong High School site mixed-income housing: delivered 2020 (first phase), ~150 units.
- Creighton Court Phase B: 72 new units scheduled for Q1 2026.
- 25th Street corridor: new supermarket opened (food-access indicator); new small businesses documented.
- No published causal evidence on health outcomes or the LE gap.
- VCU Center on Society and Health: Richmond east-west LE gap quantified at 20-30 years depending on tract (Woolf et al. 2015-present).

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** Bon Secours' explicit framing of the corridor as a health-outcomes geography, not merely housing or economic development, is a best-practice framing IMAN shares. Supermarket attraction and mixed-income redevelopment are measurable. Pace is slow but deliberate.
**Failed / limits:** Decade-plus lag between school closure and mixed-income replacement (2004-2020 Armstrong) is a cautionary lesson on redevelopment timelines. Legacy-resident return rates on RRHA redevelopment sites have been mixed and contested. Health-outcome attribution has never been formally established.

## Transferability to Englewood
**Unusually close structural analogue** (Catholic/Christian hospital health-anchored corridor ↔ Muslim-rooted IMAN FQHC corridor; closed schools turned into housing). Best informs: (1) Regenerator timeline — IMAN's 2026 ground-break on a 2003-closed school is roughly the same pace Richmond achieved with Armstrong; the lesson is that once committed, execute fast. (2) Bon Secours anchor-partner framing is a playbook Northwestern Memorial and IMAN can model. (3) Pair IMAN's work with Woolf et al.'s published Richmond LE-gap research as a citation template for the Englewood-Streeterville gap — both cities have a published 20-30-year LE gap with institutional validation.

# Jeremiah Program — Two-Generation (Single Mothers + Children)

## Location & demographics at start
Campuses in Minneapolis, St. Paul, Austin, Fargo, Boston, Brooklyn, Rochester (MN), Baltimore. Target population: low-income single mothers pursuing college degrees, with young children. Typical participant enters at ~27 years old, with one to two children; incomes below 185% federal poverty line; predominantly women of color (Black and Latina). Not a neighborhood-place-based model per se — a two-generation intervention layered onto disinvested urban neighborhoods.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
Founded 1998 in Minneapolis. Current annual operating budget >$20M across 9 campuses. Funders: Ascend at the Aspen Institute, Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, W.K. Kellogg Foundation, individual major donors, campus-specific local funders.

## Intervention components
Residential two-generation model: (1) Safe, affordable on-campus housing for mother and children (2-4 years); (2) quality on-site early-childhood education (birth to age 5); (3) individualized support (empowerment and life skills, financial literacy); (4) college coaching toward career-track associate or bachelor's degrees; (5) supportive peer community.

## Evaluation design strength
Internal program data + Wilder Research ROI study (2013). **No RCT.** Ascend at the Aspen Institute has catalogued the program as a leading two-generation model. Boston model documented by Brandeis PERG (Policy Evaluation Research Group).

## Measured outcomes
- **College graduation rate: 68% of Jeremiah participants graduate from college** — vs. national single-mother college graduation rate <13% (Jeremiah Program reported).
- **Average annual wage of graduates (past 5 years): ~$48,000.**
- **77% of graduates report decreased reliance on public assistance.**
- Children: ~90% of children in Jeremiah child-development centers perform above age-development benchmarks; 81% of child alumni are performing at or above grade level in elementary/middle school.
- ROI (Wilder Research 2013): every $1 in private investment generates ~$4 in social return.
- These are self-reported / Wilder-partnered outcomes without independent counterfactual; likely overstate pure program effect due to selection.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** Durable, scaled, well-managed two-generation program with demonstrably higher college completion than the single-mother national average. Strong child-outcome data. Housing + childcare + coaching is a cohesive theory of change.
**Failed / limits:** Self-selection is substantial — applicants who commit to a 2-4 year residential program are not representative. No RCT. Program scale relative to need is tiny (<500 families per year nationally). Limited evidence on life-expectancy or mortality outcomes directly.

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) **A supportive-housing model IMAN has not considered.** Regenerator currently plans supportive housing for returning citizens; a Jeremiah-style women-and-children wing (IMAN has flagged "dedicated women's housing unit" in its profile) could adopt the two-generation framework. (2) Partnership with Kennedy King College (Fresh Boost academic cohort) aligns with Jeremiah's college-pipeline model — an academic anchor is already in place. (3) The Wilder ROI framework (4:1) is a defensible SROI citation; IMAN could adapt the methodology.

# Detroit Future City + Michigan Central (Corktown)

## Location & demographics at start
Detroit Future City (DFC) is a citywide strategic framework (2012) for Detroit after its 2013 bankruptcy. Michigan Central is a site-specific adaptive-reuse redevelopment in Corktown, Detroit. Corktown baseline (2015): ~1,400 residents; diverse but historically white/Irish; adjacent to majority-Black neighborhoods. Wider Detroit baseline: ~80% Black, poverty rate ~35%, median household income $30k, life expectancy in majority-Black tracts 72-75 years. Corktown itself is less Englewood-analogous; the corporate-anchor-led redevelopment model and adjacent-neighborhood spillovers are the relevant benchmark.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
Detroit Future City founding 2012; now an independent nonprofit think-and-do tank. Michigan Central: Ford Motor Company acquired Michigan Central Station 2018; $950M restoration completed 2024. Michigan Central mobility-innovation district 30 acres; Newlab startup incubator; partnerships with Google, University of Michigan.

## Intervention components
**DFC:** (1) 50-year land-use vision for Detroit's 140 square miles; (2) Open-Space Network Plan; (3) Working with Lots program (repurpose vacant lots productively); (4) workforce and green-infrastructure pipelines. **Michigan Central:** (1) Adaptive reuse of historic train depot; (2) Newlab startup accelerator (100+ mobility-tech startups); (3) Code Next Detroit (Google free computer-science education for Black, Latinx, Native American high-schoolers); (4) Joe Louis Greenway trail system linking Corktown to Black neighborhoods.

## Evaluation design strength
Case studies and journalism. DFC has published its own outcome reports. **No independent peer-reviewed causal evaluation.** Corktown property-value data is publicly available and has been analyzed in several trade publications (not peer-reviewed).

## Measured outcomes
- **Corktown median home price: $215,000 (2018) → $500,000+ (2024-25)** for renovated historic homes.
- Corktown residential property values appreciating at **~2x the citywide Detroit average** over 2019-2024.
- Newlab: 100+ startups hosted; collectively raised "nearly $700,000 in venture capital" per public reporting (this figure from search is suspiciously low — likely typo for $700M; IMAN team should verify before citing).
- Joe Louis Greenway: multi-phase construction in progress; segments opened 2022-2024.
- Adjacent Black neighborhoods (North Corktown, Core City): early-stage gentrification pressure documented.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** Michigan Central demonstrates corporate-anchor adaptive reuse at heroic scale. Adjacent benefits (Greenway, Code Next, small business) extend beyond the core site. Ford's commitment creates 10+ year patient capital.
**Failed / risks:** Classic amenity-led-gentrification pattern — property values in Corktown up sharply, displacement pressure on long-term residents. Benefits to adjacent majority-Black neighborhoods (Core City, North Corktown) mostly framed as "coming" rather than measured. Corporate-anchor model concentrates governance power with Ford.

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) **Adaptive reuse at neighborhood scale** — the Regenerator (closed-school-to-housing) is the small-dollar Englewood version of the Michigan Central adaptive-reuse thesis. (2) **Anchor framing risk** — Corktown demonstrates that a corporate anchor, without CLT/right-of-return protections, produces gentrification. IMAN should structure Northwestern Memorial's co-investment explicitly to avoid this trap. (3) Code Next-style corporate partnership for youth STEM — Weekend Warriors could partner with a Chicago tech anchor (Google Chicago, Salesforce Tower) for free CS education.

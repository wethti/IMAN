# Cure Violence (original Chicago CeaseFire) — Chicago

## Location & demographics at start
West and South Side Chicago — specific sites including West Garfield Park, Logan Square, Southwest, Auburn Gresham, Englewood, and others. Originally launched in the Near West Side; expanded to 25+ Chicago neighborhoods at peak. Target communities: majority Black or Hispanic, poverty rates 25-45%, gun-violence rates among the highest in the United States. Englewood was a Cure Violence site from 2004 onward.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
Founded 1999-2000 by Gary Slutkin (University of Illinois at Chicago School of Public Health). Known as CeaseFire-Chicago (2000-2012), rebranded Cure Violence Global ~2012. State of Illinois, Joyce Foundation, Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, various federal and city sources. Funding fluctuated sharply with state budget cycles (2015-2017 Illinois budget impasse nearly terminated the program).

## Intervention components
Public-health model treating violence as contagious disease. Core roles: (1) **Violence interrupters** — credible messengers, often formerly incarcerated, mediate conflicts on the street before retaliation; (2) **Outreach workers** — sustained relationship with highest-risk individuals, link to services; (3) **Community mobilization** — public marches, vigils after shootings, norm-change messaging. Intentionally not partnered with police; strict information-wall to preserve interrupter credibility.

## Evaluation design strength
Multiple independent evaluations of mixed quality. **Skogan et al. 2008** — National Institute of Justice evaluation of 7 Chicago CeaseFire sites using time-series, hot-spot mapping, and network analysis; quasi-experimental. **Webster et al. 2013** — evaluation of Baltimore Safe Streets (Cure Violence replication) in AJPH. **Butts et al. 2015** Annual Review of Public Health systematic review. 2024 arXiv quasi-experimental analysis of NYC Cure Violence (Delgado, Swanson et al.).

## Measured outcomes
- **Skogan 2008 (Chicago):** In 4 of 7 CeaseFire sites, statistically significant reductions in the number of shots (gun-related batteries/assaults) compared with matched comparison areas; 3 sites showed no effect.
- **Webster 2013 (Baltimore Safe Streets):** Reductions in killings of up to 56% and in shootings of up to 44% in 4 program sites; strong evidence of norm change (rejecting use of violence).
- **Delgado 2024 (NYC):** Quasi-experimental evidence of significant shooting reductions in NYC Cure Violence zones relative to comparison areas.
- **Butts 2015 systematic review:** Evidence base "promising but mixed" — effects vary by site, fidelity, and local conditions. Not yet rated "effective" by some clearinghouses due to heterogeneity.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** The violence-as-contagion framework became foundational in U.S. public-health violence prevention. Credible-messenger model is the proven mechanism. Where implemented with fidelity (Baltimore Safe Streets) and sustained funding, effect sizes are large.
**Failed:** Fidelity varies dramatically across sites. Several Chicago sites closed during the 2015-2017 state budget impasse, with documented upticks in violence in those neighborhoods. The program struggled to scale measurement discipline. Some NIJ-rated evaluations found null effects.

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) IMAN's Community Engagement & Safety Team — currently not structured as formal violence interruption. IMAN could partner with Cure Violence Chicago (or its successor Institute for Nonviolence Chicago) to add a formal interrupter component with trained credible messengers. (2) **READI Chicago is the upgrade** — longer dosage, structured CBT, evaluated at RCT scale. IMAN should consider Cure Violence for immediate low-cost de-escalation layer and READI-style for sustained 18-month dosage on highest-risk individuals. (3) Cure Violence's Baltimore evidence provides a defensible per-dollar mortality-reduction citation for grant proposals.

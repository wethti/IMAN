# Skid Row Housing Trust (SRHT) — Los Angeles

## Location & demographics at start
Downtown Los Angeles Skid Row and surrounding neighborhoods. Target population: single adults experiencing chronic homelessness, severe mental illness, substance-use disorders. At organizational peak (2010s), served ~2,000 residents across 25+ buildings. Skid Row tract demographics: majority Black and Latino, near-100% poverty among unhoused population, severe health and mortality burden (Skid Row adult LE among lowest in U.S., estimated <60 years for chronically homeless subpopulation). Serves extreme end of Englewood's spectrum.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
1989–2023 (organization dissolved 2023 amid financial failure). Built and operated ~29 permanent supportive housing buildings. Annual operating budget $25-40M at peak. Funders: federal HUD HOME and CoC, California Mental Health Services Act, LA County, Conrad N. Hilton Foundation, California Community Foundation.

## Intervention components
Permanent Supportive Housing (PSH) model — "housing first": low-barrier entry, no sobriety requirement, integrated on-site case management, behavioral-health services, primary care linkage. Architecturally innovative — SRHT commissioned award-winning designs from Michael Maltzan and others to de-stigmatize homeless housing.

## Evaluation design strength
Mixed. Housing First overall has strong RCT evidence (Canada's At Home/Chez Soi, N=2,148, 2008-2013; Tsemberis/Pathways to Housing). SRHT-specific evaluation is limited to self-reported metrics and 2023 Hilton Foundation lessons-learned report after the organization's dissolution. **No peer-reviewed SRHT-specific mortality evaluation.**

## Measured outcomes (SRHT and PSH generally)
- **Housing First RCTs: 80-90% housing retention at 24 months** among chronically homeless adults (Tsemberis et al. 2004 American Journal of Public Health; Aubry et al. 2015 Canadian At Home/Chez Soi, N=2,148).
- **Mortality: 37% reduction in mortality** among chronically homeless adults placed into PSH vs. usual care (Henwood et al. 2015, various U.S. and Canadian cohorts).
- Emergency-room utilization: 40-60% reductions post-housing placement (Larimer et al. 2009 JAMA, Housing First for chronic public inebriates).
- Cost offsets: $4,000-$40,000 annual savings per resident in health, criminal-justice, shelter costs (Culhane et al. 2002 and subsequent LA replication studies).
- SRHT-specific: ~2,000 residents housed annually at peak; retention rates broadly consistent with Housing First benchmarks.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** SRHT demonstrated scale PSH is operationally feasible in the most difficult U.S. urban homelessness context. Contributed to Housing First's mainstream adoption nationally. Architectural innovation shaped PSH design.
**Failed:** Organization collapsed in 2023 due to deferred-maintenance and operating-cost crisis — a warning that PSH requires sustainable operating subsidy, not just capital subsidy. A 2023 Hilton Foundation "Redesign Required" report is required reading for any org contemplating PSH operation.

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) **Regenerator design** — the 48-unit supportive housing project in the Regenerator should adopt Housing First principles (low-barrier entry, integrated on-site services). (2) **Operating-cost discipline** — SRHT's collapse is a warning that capital finance alone does not sustain PSH; IMAN needs a committed 20-year operating subsidy stream (state MHSA-equivalent, Medicaid 1115 waiver, or philanthropic endowment). (3) The peer-reviewed Housing First mortality effect (−37%) is a defensible number for IMAN to cite in LE-impact modeling for the Regenerator population.

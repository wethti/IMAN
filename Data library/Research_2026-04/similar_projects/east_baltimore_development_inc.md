# East Baltimore Development Inc. (EBDI) — Johns Hopkins-led

## Location & demographics at start
88-acre redevelopment area in Middle East neighborhood of East Baltimore, adjacent to Johns Hopkins Hospital and medical campus. 2001 baseline: ~2,000 rowhouses (most deteriorated, many occupied), predominantly Black residents, poverty rate ~40%, median household income ~$15-20k, life expectancy ~65 years. Highly Englewood-analogous.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
Founded 2003. Active through present as a redevelopment authority. Total investment >$1 billion through 2024 (Johns Hopkins, City of Baltimore, State of Maryland, Annie E. Casey Foundation, federal HUD/NIH/EPA). Successive master plans (2001, 2006, 2020s) revised as community pushback and funder skepticism grew.

## Intervention components
"Science and Technology Park at Johns Hopkins" biotech district plus mixed-income housing redevelopment. Components: (1) demolish ~2,000 rowhouses via eminent domain; (2) relocate residents with purportedly first-in-class relocation package; (3) rebuild with mixed-income rental and owner-occupied housing, life-sciences office, charter school (Henderson-Hopkins K-8), park, hotel; (4) anchor-institution hiring and local-procurement commitments.

## Evaluation design strength
Case study / journalism / critical academic literature. Marisela Gomez's book *Race, Class, Power, and Organizing in East Baltimore* (Lexington, 2012) is the most comprehensive scholarly account. Johns Hopkins News-Letter has published investigative reporting. Baltimore Sun documentation of return rates. **No peer-reviewed mortality or LE outcome evaluation.**

## Measured outcomes
- ~800 Black and Brown families displaced from demolished rowhouses.
- **Fewer than 1 in 10 displaced residents returned or planned to return to the redeveloped Eager Park neighborhood** (Baltimore Sun 2018 investigation).
- Fewer than a quarter of displaced residents expressed interest in returning.
- Mixed-income Eager Park housing completed; biotech office campus partially built with far fewer tenants than projected.
- Local-hire commitments: 490 workers retained employment on EBDI projects; ~40% from Baltimore (161 minority), ~25% from East Baltimore (107 minority) — below original projections.
- Life expectancy and health outcomes in surrounding tracts: no independent evaluation.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** Henderson-Hopkins charter school opened and functions; Eager Park has green space where rowhouses once stood; biotech tenants include partial occupancy of Science+Technology Park.
**Failed — severely:** EBDI is widely cited in the community-development literature as a cautionary example. Eminent domain was used against occupied, owned homes in a majority-Black neighborhood to benefit a wealthy academic medical anchor. Relocation promises were poorly executed. Right-of-return was not honored at scale. Biotech campus did not meet tenanting or employment targets. Trust with the surrounding community is deeply damaged — an opportunity cost that will take generations to rebuild.

## Transferability to Englewood
**Critical negative benchmark.** Best informs: (1) What NOT to do on IMAN's corridor lots and Regenerator — never use eminent domain against occupied homes; never rely on a single-anchor biotech-style displacement logic. (2) Right-of-return must be legally binding and community-verified, not a policy aspiration. (3) Northwestern Memorial partnership — IMAN's co-invest logic is good, but IMAN should explicitly contrast itself with EBDI and preempt the "Hopkins of the South Side" framing. (4) The East Baltimore story is a powerful narrative tool for investor conversations: IMAN's community-rooted land ownership is precisely the structural difference.

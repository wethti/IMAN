# New Orleans Redevelopment Authority (NORA) — Lower Ninth Ward & citywide post-Katrina

## Location & demographics at start
New Orleans post-Hurricane Katrina (2005). Lower Ninth Ward baseline (2000): ~20,000 residents, ~98% Black, majority working-class homeowners (highest Black homeownership rate of any U.S. neighborhood at baseline), median household income ~$27k, poverty rate ~30%. Post-Katrina: 80% of homes destroyed. Other NORA target areas: Central City, Treme, Hollygrove. Englewood-analogous on race, disinvestment, and disaster-disinvestment compounding.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
NORA founded 1968; post-Katrina redevelopment mandate from 2005 onward. Primary post-Katrina funding: HUD CDBG-DR (Disaster Recovery), State of Louisiana Road Home program, City of New Orleans, FEMA, private foundations. Aggregate post-Katrina federal, state, and philanthropic investment in New Orleans >$140 billion (all categories).

## Intervention components
(1) **Land acquisition and assembly** — NORA took title to thousands of abandoned properties post-Katrina (via Road Home voluntary buyout program); (2) **Lot Next Door program** — sold blighted lots to adjacent homeowners at deep discount (often $1-$100); (3) **RFPs for affordable housing development**; (4) blight reduction; (5) more recently, commercial corridor development (Lower Ninth Ward grocery store).

## Evaluation design strength
Brookings Institution and Urban Institute have published multiple evaluations of post-Katrina New Orleans recovery. Brookings 2025 "Focusing on vacant properties in New Orleans" (Nelson). **No causal RCT/quasi-experimental NORA-specific evaluation**, but strong descriptive / comparative evidence.

## Measured outcomes
- **Lower Ninth Ward population recovery: only 36% of 2000 population by 2019-2023** (Brookings 2025) — meaning ~64% of the baseline population never returned.
- Only ~20% of Lower Ninth's original 20,000 residents returned.
- Houses in Lower Ninth Ward cost far more to build than they sell for; ~$75,000 in subsidies often needed to close the gap.
- NORA land disposition: thousands of parcels transferred via Lot Next Door; affordable-housing RFPs produced hundreds of units.
- Citywide: New Orleans population still ~15% below 2000 level as of 2020 Census; Black population disproportionately did not return.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** NORA's Lot Next Door program is a nationally cited innovation — inexpensive, community-controlled, low-overhead tool for blight reduction and neighborhood stabilization. Land banking infrastructure survived and continues to function.
**Failed — catastrophically for displaced residents:** Despite massive federal investment, the Lower Ninth Ward was never rebuilt at scale; original Black homeowners were displaced. The federal Road Home formula systematically undercompensated Black homeowners (formula tied to pre-storm property value, which reflected redlining-era disinvestment). The Lower Ninth is a textbook case of disaster-driven displacement.

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) **Land banking and Lot Next Door.** Englewood has high vacancy; a Chicago City Council-authorized Lot Next Door equivalent (sold at deep discount to adjacent owners) is an evidence-based, low-cost tool IMAN could champion. (2) **Federal-formula skepticism.** The Road Home/Lower Ninth story is a warning that federal-formula-based recovery systematically under-serves disinvested Black neighborhoods. Any federal-leveraged IMAN project must interrogate formula equity. (3) Population-return tracking: IMAN should build a population-return indicator into the 2040 dashboard (a direct analogue to the Brookings "36%" figure). (4) Grocery store as corridor anchor — Lower Ninth's new grocery is a direct echo of Fresh Market.

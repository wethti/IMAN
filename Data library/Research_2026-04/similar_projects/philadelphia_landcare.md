# Philadelphia LandCare (Pennsylvania Horticultural Society vacant-lot greening)

## Location & demographics at start
Philadelphia, PA, primarily North, West, and Southwest sections — historically redlined, majority-Black tracts with median household incomes in the bottom quintile of the MSA, poverty rates >30%, vacancy rates >15%. Study area encompassed ~40,000 vacant parcels citywide. Tracts in the randomized trial were majority non-white with high poverty and high baseline gun-violence rates. Englewood-analogous.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
PHS LandCare program launched ~1996, ongoing. Treats ~11,000 lots across Philadelphia. Per-lot remediation cost ~$1,600 (2013 dollars); annual maintenance ~$180/lot. Funders: City of Philadelphia, Pennsylvania Horticultural Society, Knight Foundation, Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, NIH (R01 evaluation grants).

## Intervention components
(1) Remove trash and debris; (2) grade the land; (3) plant new grass and a small number of trees to create a parklike setting; (4) install low wooden perimeter fences; (5) regular maintenance (contracted to local landscapers, often from same neighborhoods). Three arms tested in 2018 RCT: full greening / mowing + trash cleanup / no intervention.

## Evaluation design strength
**Gold standard: citywide cluster-randomized controlled trial.** Branas CC, South E, Kondo MC, et al. "Citywide cluster randomized trial to restore blighted vacant land and its effects on violence, crime, and fear." PNAS 115(12):2946-2951, March 2018. 541 vacant lots randomized into 110 clusters. Precursor trial: Branas et al. "Greening vacant lots to reduce violent crime: a randomised controlled trial." Injury Prevention, 2013.

## Measured outcomes
- Overall crime in treated clusters: **−13.3% vs. control (95% CI: −22.4% to −4.2%), P=0.004** (Branas 2018).
- Gun assaults in treatment clusters: **−29.1% (95% CI: −49.7% to −8.4%), P=0.006** (Branas 2018 — this is the frequently cited "29%" figure).
- Shootings specifically: greening intervention **−6.8% and mowing-cleanup −9.2%** (subsequent AJPH paper: South et al. 2018, "Effect of Remediating Blighted Vacant Land on Shootings," AJPH 109(1):140-144).
- Residents' self-reported fear of going outside: reduced 58% in greening arm.
- Branas et al. also documented mental-health benefits (depression symptoms reduced) for residents near greened lots (South, Hohl, Kondo, MacDonald, Branas 2018, JAMA Network Open 1(3):e180298).

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** Low-cost, low-tech intervention with durable, independently replicated reductions in violent crime, gun violence, and mental-health symptoms. Most rigorous place-based causal evidence in the benchmark set — RCT at scale.
**Limits:** Effects are localized (on and immediately near the lot); does not address underlying structural drivers of violence. Maintenance is ongoing cost — programs have failed in other cities when maintenance funding lapses.

## Transferability to Englewood
**Directly transferable — and IMAN already owns the inputs.** Best informs: (1) IMAN's corridor lots (currently vacant, planned for garden/recreation/development) — even before ultimate development, a LandCare-style treatment would produce measurable violence and mental-health benefits. Englewood has among the highest vacant-lot densities in Chicago. (2) Griot Plaza expansion logic. (3) Partner leverage — Chicago has a parallel Large Lots program; collaboration with City of Chicago could scale. IMAN can quote the 29% gun-assault reduction with 95% CI directly to funders.

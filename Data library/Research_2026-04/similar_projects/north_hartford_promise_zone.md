# North Hartford Promise Zone — Hartford, CT

## Location & demographics at start
3.11 square miles, Clay Arsenal / Northeast / Upper Albany neighborhoods of Hartford. 2015 baseline: ~25,000 residents, ~70% Black + Latino (roughly equal), poverty rate ~42%, median household income ~$23k, life expectancy ~69-73 years (per CT DPH tract data). First New England city designated as a federal Promise Zone (2015). Englewood-analogous on race, poverty, historical disinvestment.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
Designated 2015 by HUD/Obama Administration. Promise Zone designation is a 10-year federal partnership status (not direct funding); access to preference points on existing federal grants, federal liaison, and AmeriCorps VISTAs. Leveraged funding: Connecticut Children's received a $30M U.S. Department of Education Promise Neighborhoods grant (2017). Additional leverage from CT Dept. of Housing, Hartford Foundation for Public Giving, LISC.

## Intervention components
(1) Federal interagency coordination on existing grant programs; (2) North End Revitalization Strategy — housing rehab, small-business support; (3) Connecticut Children's Promise Neighborhoods: cradle-to-career education pipeline modeled on HCZ; (4) blight reduction and vacant-property initiatives (Liberal Arts Action Lab mapping project, 2018); (5) VISTA and AmeriCorps volunteer corps.

## Evaluation design strength
Connecticut Children's Promise Neighborhoods has a formal evaluation plan with quasi-experimental design (pre-test/post-test and comparison groups). Promise Zone designation itself has no direct evaluation mechanism; HUD does not fund a central evaluation. **No peer-reviewed outcome evaluation published as of 2025.**

## Measured outcomes
- Secured federal, state, and philanthropic investments leveraged via Promise Zone status (aggregate estimated >$100M across agencies, per Hartford/Murphy office self-reporting).
- Connecticut Children's $30M Promise Neighborhoods grant (2017) directly attributable to Promise Zone infrastructure.
- Process indicators (partner organizations engaged, residents served) reported; population-level outcome data for the 3.11 sq mi area is sparse.
- Hartford's violent-crime and poverty rates have moved modestly but the counterfactual is unestablished.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** Promise Zone designation did measurably catalyze federal grant flows — the Promise Neighborhoods award is a concrete outcome. The designation creates a focal geography that philanthropic and government partners can rally around. Low-cost federal intervention (~$0 direct funding) yielding real leverage.
**Failed:** Without direct Promise Zone funding, outcomes depend entirely on grant-leverage capacity, which varies across cities. North Hartford has not produced documented population-level improvements on poverty, employment, or LE comparable to flagship Promise Neighborhoods (Northside Achievement Zone, Berea). Program is politically fragile — Promise Zones nearly eliminated in 2017-2020 federal budgets.

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) Opportunity Zones / federal designations strategy — Englewood is already in Opportunity Zone and Promise Neighborhoods-eligible geography. IMAN could pursue a Promise Zone-analog designation (if Congress reauthorizes) for catalytic leverage. (2) Connecticut Children's model — hospital-led Promise Neighborhoods grant is a template IMAN could co-apply for with Northwestern. (3) The honest lesson: designations without direct funding are limited leverage; IMAN's existing direct-service density is a greater asset than a federal label.

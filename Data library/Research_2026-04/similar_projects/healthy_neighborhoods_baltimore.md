# Healthy Neighborhoods Inc. (HNI) — Baltimore

## Location & demographics at start
42 target neighborhoods across Baltimore — "middle neighborhoods" on the edge between stable and declining: Belair-Edison, Coldstream Homestead Montebello, Patterson Park, Waverly, Medfield, Reservoir Hill, Belair-Parkside, and others. Baltimore baseline: ~600,000 residents; target neighborhoods typically 50-90% Black, median household income $30-50k, homeownership rates 40-60%, vacancy rates 5-20%. Englewood-analogous on race and disinvestment; middle-neighborhoods framing differs.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
Founded 2001 by Baltimore Community Foundation, Goldseker Foundation, and Abell Foundation. Active through present. Primary funders: Goldseker Foundation, Abell Foundation, Baltimore Community Foundation, State of Maryland, Baltimore City, participating banks (Wells Fargo, M&T, PNC). Hundreds of millions in below-market mortgage capital deployed.

## Intervention components
(1) **Healthy Block Pilots** — neighborhood-driven planning on 3-5 target blocks per neighborhood; (2) **Purchase-rehab mortgages** — below-market 30-year loans covering up to 110% of after-renovation value, 1 point below market rate, no PMI, 620 credit-score floor; (3) **Matching rehab grants** up to $10,000; (4) **Live Near Your Work** employer-contribution stacking; (5) branding and marketing campaigns for target neighborhoods.

## Evaluation design strength
Independent quasi-experimental evaluation — Reinvestment Fund (2009, 2012, 2017) using matched-tract methods compared housing prices, sales velocity, and vacancy trends in HNI target blocks vs. non-HNI comparison blocks. Fannie Mae and Goldseker Foundation commissioned follow-ups. **No RCT; no direct health-outcome evaluation.**

## Measured outcomes
- **Home price appreciation in HNI blocks: 30-40% higher than comparison blocks** over 2003-2012 (Reinvestment Fund).
- Sales velocity in HNI blocks higher; vacancy rates declined more rapidly in HNI blocks.
- Over 2,000 purchase-rehab loans originated through the HNI pipeline 2001-2019.
- Concentration effect: HNI found block-scale concentration (5-10 contiguous rehabbed homes) produced substantially larger neighborhood-level stabilization than scattered rehabs — a design finding.
- No directly measured health or mortality outcomes.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** HNI demonstrated that targeted below-market mortgage capital, concentrated geographically, stabilizes middle-disinvested Black neighborhoods without displacing legacy residents. Crucially, the model *preserves* rather than redevelops — avoiding the East Baltimore / Choice Neighborhoods displacement risks. Durable 20+ year track record with multiple independent evaluations.
**Failed / limits:** Model works best in "middle" neighborhoods, less well in the most deeply distressed ones where market demand is too low to respond to below-market capital. Does not directly address low-income renters. Health-outcome effects inferred but not measured.

## Transferability to Englewood
**High relevance — this is a model IMAN could add on corridor blocks not covered by Regenerator.** Best informs: (1) A partnership between IMAN, Neighborhood Housing Services of Chicago (local HNI analog), and a mortgage partner (Northwestern MPIC-equivalent) could deploy HNI-style below-market purchase-rehab mortgages on 63rd Street corridor blocks. (2) Concentration finding — IMAN's Go Green Development cluster around 63rd/Racine already reflects this logic. (3) Live Near Your Work employer-contribution model maps directly onto Northwestern Memorial + IMAN FQHC partnership (Greater University Circle parallel).

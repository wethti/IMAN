# Mission Asset Fund (MAF) — San Francisco Mission District

## Location & demographics at start
Mission District, San Francisco. Baseline (2007): ~56% Latino, ~20% white, ~5% Black, ~15% Asian; median household income $50-60k (lower than SF-wide $80k+); high share of unbanked / underbanked households; large Mexican and Central American immigrant population. Target population: financially excluded individuals nationwide (eventually). Englewood-analogous on: financial exclusion, poverty proxy, majority-minority; different on racial composition (primarily Latino vs. Englewood's Black majority).

## Years active, budget, primary funders
2007 present. Founded by José Quiñonez (MacArthur Fellow, 2016). Annual budget $5-10M. Funders: Citi Foundation, JPMorgan Chase Foundation, Ford Foundation, Kresge Foundation, W.K. Kellogg Foundation, U.S. Treasury CDFI Fund.

## Intervention components
"Lending Circles" — formalized, zero-interest peer lending groups based on the Mexican *tanda* rotating-savings tradition. MAF reports the payments to credit bureaus, converting informal community lending into credit-score-building transactions. Additional: financial-education curriculum, immigration-fee loans, COVID relief grants (MAF deployed ~$50M in cash grants during COVID), scholarships for undocumented students.

## Evaluation design strength
**Independent quasi-experimental evaluation.** San Francisco State University's César Chávez Institute (Hererra, Silvermann, et al.) evaluated Lending Circles over 2 years, N=600+ participants across 5 Bay Area communities. Pre/post credit-score analysis; comparison groups not formal RCT but with credit-bureau data baseline-adjusted. Published as an internal evaluation report and featured in Financial Health Network research; peer-reviewed journal coverage in 2017-2019.

## Measured outcomes
- **Average FICO credit-score increase: +168 points over 2 years** (Cesar Chavez Institute evaluation).
- **9 of 10 clients without a credit score established one for the first time.**
- Average debt reduction per participant: $1,000.
- Financial-education add-on: additional +27 credit-score points.
- Participants unlocked average of 3 additional credit tradelines.
- Interest savings: ~$360 per participant (from transition to mainstream credit products).
- Expanded to 50+ nonprofit partners across 17 U.S. states.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** Lending Circles are one of the clearest success stories in U.S. financial inclusion. Built on a culturally authentic tradition, formalized into credit-bureau infrastructure. Peer-reviewed, replicable, and scaling. Credit-score gains of +168 points are transformative — they change mortgage, car, and rental access.
**Failed / limits:** Dependent on culturally cohesive peer groups; harder to replicate where social-trust infrastructure is weaker. Credit-score gain does not by itself produce employment or housing — it unlocks access but does not create demand-side opportunities. Not designed for mortality outcomes.

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) **A new IMAN initiative gap.** IMAN has no financial-inclusion program. Lending Circles could be added as a direct service with low startup cost (~$250k seed) and documented outcomes. IMAN's Muslim community tradition of *qard hasan* (interest-free lending) has near-identical structure to tandas. (2) Credit-score improvement is a proven upstream lever for housing stability — complements Regenerator pipeline. (3) MAF is a generous technical-assistance partner; IMAN could become one of its 50+ replication partners.

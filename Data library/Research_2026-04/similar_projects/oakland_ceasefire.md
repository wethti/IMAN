# Oakland Ceasefire — Group Violence Reduction Strategy

## Location & demographics at start
Oakland, CA. ~420,000 residents at program start (2013). Targeted census block groups in East and West Oakland with the highest gun-homicide rates. Those tracts: majority Black and Hispanic, median household income bottom quintile of the Bay Area MSA, poverty rates >25-40%, high unemployment. Clearly Englewood-analogous on race, poverty, and violence.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
Oakland Ceasefire (relaunched): February 2013 through present, though funding and leadership attention have fluctuated. Funding through City of Oakland general fund, Measure Z / Measure NN public safety dollars, California Violence Prevention and Intervention Grant Program. Program evaluation contract at John Jay College / Northeastern.

## Intervention components
Group Violence Reduction Strategy (GVRS) / focused-deterrence model (adapted from David Kennedy, Boston Ceasefire). Components: (1) identification of highest-risk groups and individuals via network analysis; (2) "call-ins" where law enforcement, community members, and service providers present a unified message — violence stops, or targeted enforcement follows; (3) intensive wraparound services (employment, behavioral health, housing) for those accepting the off-ramp; (4) community engagement and "life coaches."

## Evaluation design strength
**Quasi-experimental with matched comparison block groups.** Braga AA, Barao LM, Zimmerman G, Douglas S, Sheppard K. "Oakland Ceasefire Evaluation Final Report to the City of Oakland." 2019. Not an RCT — the strongest feasible design for place-based violence interventions. NIJ CrimeSolutions rates the program "Effective."

## Measured outcomes
- **Gun homicides: 31.5% reduction city-wide, 2013-2017, associated with Ceasefire (controlling for other trends and seasonal variations)** (Braga et al. 2019).
- Gang/group-member-involved shootings: yearly mean fell from 346.0 (2010-2012) to 196.6 (2013-2017) — a **43.2% reduction**.
- Treatment block groups vs. matched comparison block groups: **20% reduction in shootings** attributable to Ceasefire focus (Braga 2019).
- 2024 reanalysis (Braga et al.) confirmed reductions persisted when Ceasefire implementation was sustained; reductions attenuated when city leadership de-emphasized the program.
- No published cost-effectiveness-per-life-saved but Oakland avoided ~150 homicides cumulatively over the 5-year period (Giffords estimate).

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** Focused deterrence with robust service off-ramp is one of the most rigorously evaluated violence-reduction strategies in the U.S. Reductions are large, durable when program is maintained, and transportable across cities (Boston, Oakland, Stockton, Indianapolis all show similar effect sizes).
**Failed:** Program fidelity is fragile — changes in police leadership, political will, or funding materially degrade outcomes within 1-2 years. Requires trusted partnership between law enforcement and community organizations, which is culturally difficult in many disinvested Black neighborhoods. Community engagement can atrophy if not deliberately maintained.

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) IMAN's current Community Engagement and Safety Team — this team performs de-escalation but is *not* dosage-based GVRS. Chicago has run GVRS-like efforts (GVI, VRS) with partial success. IMAN could partner with Chicago Partnership for Safe & Peaceful Communities to add a formal focused-deterrence component. (2) The call-in model explicitly requires community messengers — IMAN's Muslim-rooted community standing makes it a natural messenger. (3) This is complementary to READI Chicago (see separate profile), not duplicative.

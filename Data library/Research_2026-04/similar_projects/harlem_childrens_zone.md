# Harlem Children's Zone (HCZ)

## Location & demographics at start
Central Harlem, New York City. Target "Zone" expanded from 24 blocks (1997) to 97 blocks (~100,000 residents, ~10,000 children). At baseline in the 1990s: ~75% Black, ~20% Hispanic; child poverty >40%; asthma hospitalization rates 5x national average; high-school graduation rates in single digits for some cohorts. Life expectancy in Central Harlem in 2006 ~75 years (NYC DOHMH) — roughly 9 years below the citywide top tract. Demographically Englewood-analogous.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
1997 present. Annual operating budget ~$120M (2023). Primary funders: Robin Hood Foundation, Goldman Sachs, Google, private individual donors. Geoffrey Canada founder; current CEO Kwame Owusu-Kesse.

## Intervention components
Cradle-to-college "pipeline": Baby College (parent prenatal), Harlem Gems (pre-K), Promise Academy charter schools (K-12), college success office, health clinic, fitness/obesity program, workforce development. Explicit geographic saturation thesis — serve 65% of children in the Zone.

## Evaluation design strength
**Lottery-based quasi-experimental (strongest natural experiment in neighborhood CDIs).** Dobbie & Fryer (AEJ: Applied Economics, 2011) used oversubscribed charter-school lotteries as quasi-random assignment. Dobbie & Fryer (2015) produced medium-term follow-up. No RCT of the full Zone wraparound package.

## Measured outcomes
- Promise Academy students gain +0.229σ per year in math and +0.047σ per year in reading (Dobbie & Fryer 2011).
- Effects in middle school sufficient to close Black–white math gap; elementary effects closed Black–white gaps in both math and ELA.
- **Critical null finding:** Students attending HCZ charter schools who lived outside the Zone boundary showed identical academic gains to those inside the Zone — implying the community-program wraparound (Baby College, health, etc.) added no measurable educational lift on top of the school itself (Dobbie & Fryer 2011, 2015).
- Medium-term (Dobbie & Fryer 2015): HCZ attendance increased college enrollment, reduced teen pregnancy and incarceration among males.
- No published causal evidence on life expectancy or adult mortality.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** High-dosage charter schooling produces large, durable academic gains for Black students in a historically disinvested neighborhood. Multi-decade patient capital. Strong brand enabled philanthropic scale.
**Failed / unproven:** The "full-pipeline wraparound" theory of change is not supported by the lottery evidence — at least for academic outcomes. Federal Promise Neighborhoods replication did not reliably reproduce HCZ results. Cost per child is extremely high; few communities can replicate the fundraising scale.

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) Weekend Warriors / youth programming — if IMAN wants academic impact, the evidence says invest in a single high-dosage school partner, not wraparound alone; (2) measurement strategy — IMAN could use oversubscribed program lotteries (Green Reentry cohort selection, Fresh Boost cohort) as natural experiments with waitlist controls; (3) caution: do not promise life-expectancy effects from wraparound alone without a school-quality lever.

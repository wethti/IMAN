# Greater University Circle Initiative (GUCI) & Greater Circle Living — Cleveland

## Location & demographics at start
Seven neighborhoods surrounding University Circle, Cleveland's medical-education-cultural district: Hough, Fairfax, Glenville, Buckeye-Shaker, Little Italy, Central, Kinsman. 2005 baseline: ~45,000 residents, >80% Black, poverty rates 35-50%, median household income $18-25k, vacancy rates >20%, life expectancy in Hough/Central tracts 64-68 years (CDC USALEEP later confirmed). Highly Englewood-analogous.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
2005 present (20+ years). Convened by Cleveland Foundation. Anchor institution financial commitments: Cleveland Clinic, University Hospitals, Case Western Reserve University — collectively tens of millions in procurement, hiring, and housing incentives. Associated Evergreen Cooperatives launched 2008 with ~$8M seed from Cleveland Foundation and anchors.

## Intervention components
Four work streams: (1) **Buy Local** — anchor procurement targets with local businesses; (2) **Hire Local** — anchor hiring pipelines from Greater University Circle neighborhoods; (3) **Live Local** — Greater Circle Living employer-assisted housing program (forgivable loans up to $20k for anchor employees buying or renting in the GUC neighborhoods); (4) **Connect Residents** — workforce and small-business pipelines (Neighborhood Connections, Evergreen Cooperatives worker-owned businesses).

## Evaluation design strength
Multi-year independent evaluation by Cleveland State University's Levin College of Urban Affairs — Year 6 and Year 7 reports publicly available. Uses pre/post metrics plus comparison-tract analysis, not RCT. Stronger evaluation trail than most community anchor-institution efforts; peer-reviewed journal coverage by Zuckerman, Harris, and colleagues.

## Measured outcomes
- **Greater Circle Living housing program:** By 2019, ~1,100 anchor employees had received housing incentives; ~$15M in incentives deployed; owner-occupancy in GUC neighborhoods rose modestly (Cleveland State Year 7 evaluation).
- **Buy Local:** Anchor local procurement increased from baseline ~$250M to $450M+ by 2019 (Cleveland State Year 7).
- **Evergreen Cooperatives:** Employs ~250 worker-owners at scaled steady state; three enterprises (laundry, greenhouse, energy). Less than original "10 cooperatives / 5,000 jobs" goal.
- No independent evaluation of life-expectancy or neighborhood mortality effects.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** Employer-assisted housing is one of the few evaluated interventions that demonstrably increased owner-occupancy in historically disinvested Black Cleveland neighborhoods. Anchor-procurement commitments produced real dollar flows. Durable 20-year trajectory.
**Failed:** Evergreen Cooperatives scaled far below initial projections — worker-owner cooperative model is harder than the theory suggested. Population and poverty trends in Hough and Central tracts improved only modestly through 2020; life-expectancy gap remains wide. Gentrification pressure rising in Fairfax.

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) Northwestern Memorial partnership — IMAN's co-investment logic with Northwestern is structurally similar to Cleveland Clinic's GUCI commitment. IMAN should push Northwestern for a Buy Local / Hire Local / Live Local framework, not just a one-off FQHC investment. (2) The anchor-hiring pipeline is a direct template for Green Reentry placements into Northwestern and other institutional employers. (3) Employer-assisted housing subsidies for hospital workers to buy homes in Englewood would anchor the Regenerator corridor.

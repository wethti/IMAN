# Camden Coalition of Healthcare Providers (hot-spotting)

## Location & demographics at start
Camden, NJ. 2010 baseline: ~77,000 residents; ~48% Black, ~47% Hispanic; poverty rate ~40%; life expectancy ~69–72 years (among lowest in NJ). Target population for the intervention: "super-utilizers" — patients in the top 1% of hospital utilization and cost, typically with multiple chronic conditions and social complexity. Englewood-analogous at the neighborhood level; the intervention itself is patient-level, not neighborhood-level.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
Camden Coalition founded 2002 by Dr. Jeffrey Brenner. Hot-spotting care-management program ran continuously from ~2007. Funders include Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, CMS/HRSA, The Atlantic Philanthropies, Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab (evaluator). Brenner won a 2013 MacArthur "Genius" Fellowship on the strength of the pre-evaluation reporting.

## Intervention components
Multi-disciplinary care team (nurse + social worker + community health worker) engaged "super-utilizer" patients after hospital discharge; home visits; medication reconciliation; SDOH navigation (SNAP, housing, transit, behavioral health). Patient-level, not place-based.

## Evaluation design strength
**Best-in-class: Randomized controlled trial.** Finkelstein, Zhou, Taubman, Doyle, "Health Care Hotspotting — A Randomized, Controlled Trial," New England Journal of Medicine 382:152–162, Jan 9, 2020. DOI 10.1056/NEJMsa1906848. N=800 patients, 180-day readmission primary outcome.

## Measured outcomes
- Primary outcome — 180-day readmission rate: **37.7% in treatment vs. 37.8% in control. Adjusted difference 0.82 percentage points (95% CI: −5.97 to 7.61), P=0.81. No statistically significant effect.** (Finkelstein 2020.)
- Secondary outcomes (number of readmissions, various time horizons, hospital days): all null.
- One positive signal: participants in the treatment arm were more likely to receive SNAP benefits (modest, statistically significant).
- Prior observational/pre-post estimates (Brenner) had suggested large reductions in utilization; RCT showed those were regression-to-the-mean, not program effect.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** The Coalition built a durable, respected care-coordination infrastructure; lessons on building trust with high-acuity patients have informed national CMS models. SNAP enrollment boost is real.
**Failed:** The original Brenner hot-spotting theory of change — that intensive care coordination would cut readmissions and save money in super-utilizers — **did not hold under a rigorous RCT.** The null result was one of the highest-profile failures in U.S. social-policy evaluation of the last decade. It demonstrates the risk of scaling programs on pre-post evidence alone.

## Transferability to Englewood
**This is the "honesty" benchmark for IMAN.** Best informs: (1) IMAN's CHW team — CHW care coordination alone should not be expected to reduce hospital readmissions in super-utilizers without upstream structural supports (stable housing, food, income). The Camden null tells us CHWs need to be embedded in a wider system (Pathways HUB Akron is the counter-example); (2) evaluation discipline — IMAN should pre-register outcomes and use comparison groups, not pre/post summaries, for headline claims; (3) food-and-medicine + CHW pairing is defensible because the mechanism is different from Camden's readmission pathway.

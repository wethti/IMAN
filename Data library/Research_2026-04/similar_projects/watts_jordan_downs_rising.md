# Watts Revitalization — Jordan Downs + Watts Rising — Los Angeles

## Location & demographics at start
Watts neighborhood of South Los Angeles. ~35,000 residents; ~70% Latino, ~30% Black (historically 90%+ Black; demographic shift late 1990s-2020s); poverty rate ~35%; median household income $30k; highly disinvested since the 1965 Watts Uprising. Jordan Downs public-housing site was built in 1944 as Navy housing, converted to public housing, by 2010s had 700 distressed units. Englewood-analogous: majority-minority, historically Black, deeply disinvested, with a long history of broken redevelopment promises.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
Jordan Downs Choice Neighborhoods implementation grant: 2016. Watts Rising (Transformative Climate Communities): 2017 $35M CA state award. Total projected Jordan Downs redevelopment: ~$1 billion. Funders: HUD Choice Neighborhoods, California Strategic Growth Council, LA County Measure H/HHH, BRIDGE Housing (primary developer), Michaels Development.

## Intervention components
**Jordan Downs (Choice Neighborhoods):** (1) One-for-one replacement of 700 public-housing units; (2) 700+ additional market-rate and affordable units; (3) on-site supermarket, retail, park, school; (4) case management and services for existing residents. **Watts Rising:** (1) Solar and energy efficiency; (2) affordable housing; (3) urban greening; (4) active-transportation infrastructure; (5) Watts WalkBike cultural trail; (6) arts / creative-placemaking.

## Evaluation design strength
Case study and self-reporting. Watts Rising submits regular evaluation reports to California Strategic Growth Council. Jordan Downs is covered in the HUD Choice Neighborhoods 2024 Urban Institute evaluation synthesis. **USC Price's "Watts Neighborhood Health Study"** has been in development to track longitudinal health outcomes in Watts — important forthcoming evaluation.

## Measured outcomes
- Jordan Downs: **soil/environmental contamination delayed redevelopment by years**; litigation and EPA remediation required. As of 2024, ~400 new units delivered (of planned 1,400+).
- Watts Rising: solar installed on 200+ single-family homes; active-transportation infrastructure (bike lanes, pedestrian improvements) completed 2020-2023.
- USC Watts Neighborhood Health Study (ongoing): baseline data collection began ~2019; outcome results forthcoming.
- Broader Watts: violent-crime rates declined 2010-2020 though LA-wide trends confound attribution.
- Affordability: Jordan Downs new units delivered at required mix; right-of-return is being honored more reliably than some earlier Choice sites (contested figures: most sources report 85%+ return rate for original tenants, though watchdog reports are lower).

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** Combining Choice Neighborhoods (housing) with Transformative Climate Communities (climate + infrastructure) stacked funding at the same site — a model for IMAN's capital-stack strategy. Right-of-return honored. Watts Neighborhood Health Study will eventually produce the peer-reviewed evaluation the field needs.
**Failed:** Environmental contamination (lead, arsenic from steel-mill legacy) produced years of delay and lawsuits — a warning to any adaptive-reuse redevelopment, including IMAN's Regenerator. Gentrification pressure beginning to emerge. Demographic shift (Black to Latino) complicates the "legacy community" frame.

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) **Capital-stack layering** — Jordan Downs combined housing + climate + transportation grants at one site. IMAN should pursue a similar stack (Choice Neighborhoods + Climate Pollution Reduction Grant + federal transit) for the 63rd/Racine corridor. (2) **Environmental due diligence** — Regenerator and any adaptive-reuse must have EPA Phase I and Phase II assessments before ground-break. (3) Watts Neighborhood Health Study is a template IMAN could replicate with a Chicago academic partner (University of Chicago CCHE, UIC SPH) for a formal health dashboard.

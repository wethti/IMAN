# Dudley Street Neighborhood Initiative (DSNI) — Roxbury, Boston

## Location & demographics at start
Dudley Triangle / Dudley Street neighborhood, Roxbury and North Dorchester, Boston. 1984 baseline: ~24,000 residents, majority Black and Cape Verdean/Latino; 30% of land (1,300+ parcels) was vacant after decades of arson, disinvestment, and illegal dumping; poverty rate ~32%; median household income roughly $15k (1980 dollars). Clearly Englewood-analogous on race, disinvestment, vacancy, and poverty.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
1984 present (40+ years). Riley Foundation seeded DSNI with ~$500k; subsequent funding from Ford Foundation, LISC, Boston Foundation, HUD. Dudley Neighbors Inc. (DNI), the community land trust (CLT), chartered 1988. Operating budget modest (~$3–5M/year); aggregate catalytic investment on 30+ CLT acres in the hundreds of millions.

## Intervention components
(1) Resident-controlled neighborhood planning (the 1987 "Declaration of Community Rights"); (2) community land trust — DSNI is the only community-led organization in U.S. history to be granted eminent-domain authority by a city (Boston Redevelopment Authority, 1988); (3) development without displacement — 226 permanently affordable homes including 77 co-op, 55 rental, 96 shared-equity; (4) urban farm, greenhouse, commercial buildings, parks; (5) youth, workforce, and organizing programs.

## Evaluation design strength
Case study and thesis literature, not RCT. The most rigorous independent analysis is Taylor Cain's MIT DUSP thesis (2015) "Mapping impact: an analysis of the Dudley Street Neighborhood Initiative Land Trust" — pre/post and comparison-area analysis of foreclosures, vacant lots, owner-occupancy, housing affordability. DSNI is also covered in the Lincoln Institute of Land Policy's CLT literature.

## Measured outcomes
- 1,300 vacant parcels at baseline reduced to near-zero with housing, farm, and green uses.
- 226 permanently affordable housing units produced under CLT stewardship; ground lease ~$49/month.
- Cain (2015): DSNI-CLT homes foreclosed at materially lower rates than comparable Boston neighborhoods during the 2008-10 crisis, consistent with national CLT foreclosure data (~8x lower foreclosure rate in the national CLT sample, Thaden 2011).
- No independent peer-reviewed health or mortality evaluation.
- Neighborhood demographics have remained majority-minority; displacement pressure lower than Boston-wide for 2010-2020 but gentrification pressure on non-CLT parcels has increased.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** Resident-led planning produced durable community ownership of land. Eminent domain authority — extraordinary in U.S. history — made land assembly feasible. The CLT legal structure delivers on the "development without displacement" promise better than any other mechanism in the U.S. benchmark set. Multi-generational staying power.
**Failed / limitations:** Cannot anchor macro-economic revitalization by itself (jobs/employment move with broader city economy). Pace of housing production is slow compared with Choice Neighborhoods or Purpose Built. Health outcomes never formally evaluated.

## Transferability to Englewood
**This is the strongest single match for IMAN's corridor lots + Regenerator logic.** Best informs: (1) Go Green Development Group's 63rd/Racine corridor — a CLT structure for IMAN-owned corridor lots would lock in permanent affordability and prevent the East Lake / Beltline displacement pattern; (2) Regenerator + future housing — use CLT ground-lease rather than conventional ownership; (3) governance — DSNI's resident-majority board is a model for IMAN's community engagement team.

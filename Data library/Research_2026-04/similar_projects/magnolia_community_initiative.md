# Magnolia Community Initiative (MCI) — Los Angeles

## Location & demographics at start
Southwest Los Angeles, 500-block / 5-square-mile catchment including Pico-Union, Westlake. ~110,000 residents; 35,000 children and youth (10,000 under age 5); ~80% Latino, ~15% Black; 65% of children in poverty; 35% obese; 40% enter kindergarten unprepared; ~40% don't graduate high school on time. Demographically distinct from majority-Black Englewood but meets criteria 2-10 of the IMAN similarity score (poverty, food access, education, disinvestment) — useful as a collective-impact benchmark.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
Launched 2008 by Children's Bureau of Southern California with partners. Active through present. Annual operating budget modest — MCI is a network backbone, not a program operator. Funders: Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, First 5 LA, Casey Family Programs, NAM (National Academy of Medicine Culture of Health grant).

## Intervention components
"Network approach to population-level change": 70+ partners (faith, nonprofit, city, private sector) coordinated around shared child-wellbeing indicators. Target population-level outcomes for ~35,000 children: school readiness, health, economic stability, safety. Lightweight backbone organization, shared data platform, resident-led wellness surveys.

## Evaluation design strength
Case study (Stanford GSB) and formative-evaluation only. **No independent peer-reviewed causal evaluation of MCI.** Self-reported process indicators (partner count, resident survey counts, event attendance). Featured in NAM Culture of Health community-driven action plans but without RCT or quasi-experimental outcome estimates.

## Measured outcomes
- Network grew from ~30 partners (2010) to 70+ (current).
- Resident wellness survey developed and deployed across 500 blocks — novel data source but outcomes not benchmarked causally.
- California Evidence-Based Clearinghouse lists MCI at low evidence rating due to absence of rigorous outcome studies.
- No measured population-level effects on kindergarten readiness, graduation, or obesity.

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** Demonstrated feasibility of a 70-partner cross-sector backbone at neighborhood scale. Developed a resident-designed wellness survey methodology IMAN could emulate. Durable through three leadership transitions.
**Failed:** Has not produced attributable population-level outcome change after 15+ years. Illustrates the central weakness of the "collective impact" model — high coordination costs without a clear mechanism for attribution. IMAN should read this as a cautionary tale on backbone-only investments.

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) Go Green Development Group coordination logic — the 5-6 partner GoGreen coalition is structurally similar to MCI; the lesson is that *coordination without a direct-service program engine does not move population outcomes*, so IMAN's direct-service density (FQHC, Fresh Market, Regenerator) is an asset MCI never had; (2) resident-designed wellness survey as a measurement tool for the 2040 plan dashboard; (3) caution on backbone overhead — keep the coalition light.

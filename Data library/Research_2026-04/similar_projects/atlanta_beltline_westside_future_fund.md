# Atlanta BeltLine + Westside Future Fund

## Location & demographics at start
BeltLine: 22-mile loop of former rail corridors encircling central Atlanta; adjacent neighborhoods include English Avenue, Vine City, Pittsburgh, Peoplestown, Adair Park (historically Black, redlined, disinvested). Westside Future Fund target: English Avenue, Vine City, Ashview Heights, Atlanta University Center — ~25,000 residents, ~90% Black, poverty rate ~40%, median household income ~$26k (2014). Englewood-analogous on race, poverty, redlining.

## Years active, budget, primary funders
BeltLine: 2005 present. Projected full buildout ~2030. Total expected investment ~$4.8 billion. Funded via Tax Allocation District (TAD), private development, Atlanta BeltLine Inc. Westside Future Fund (WFF): founded 2014 by Atlanta's corporate community; funded by Arthur M. Blank Family Foundation, Coca-Cola Foundation, Mercedes-Benz USA, individual CEOs.

## Intervention components
BeltLine: trail + transit + parks + affordable-housing set-aside + art corridor. Westside Future Fund: anti-displacement tax fund (covers property-tax increases for qualifying legacy homeowners), Legacy Resident Retention Program, home-repair grants, affordable-housing development, Quality-of-Life Framework (community health, safety, cradle-to-career education, mixed-income).

## Evaluation design strength
Synthetic-control / quasi-experimental studies on housing values; self-reported program metrics on anti-displacement. **No peer-reviewed causal evaluation of health or mortality outcomes.** Georgia State University and Stonybrook analyses of Atlanta's Anti-Displacement Tax Fund.

## Measured outcomes
- BeltLine adjacent property values rose 17.8%–26.6% more than comparison Atlanta areas, 2011-2015 (Immergluck & Balan 2018, *Urban Geography*).
- Westside anti-displacement tax fund: ~100 legacy homeowners retained; average home appreciation ~$50,000 during program tenure (WFF-reported).
- BeltLine affordable-housing set-aside (originally 5,600 units by 2030): fell behind schedule; only ~2,500 units produced by 2022.
- Displacement: West-side neighborhoods adjacent to BeltLine lost an estimated 3,700 Black residents (2010-2020) while gaining ~2,000 white residents (census-derived estimates, Atlanta Regional Commission).

## What worked / what failed
**Worked:** BeltLine produced a celebrated trail-and-transit amenity with documented property-value lift. WFF anti-displacement tax fund is one of the only program mechanisms in the U.S. to directly compensate legacy owners for rising tax burden — worth IMAN's attention.
**Failed:** BeltLine failed to deliver on affordable-housing and transit commitments at the originally promised scale and pace. Net displacement in adjacent Black neighborhoods is one of the most cited failures of amenity-led "green gentrification." Anti-displacement measures arrived late.

## Transferability to Englewood
Best informs: (1) Green Line reopening + Griot Plaza corridor logic — amenity-led redevelopment produces gentrification risk unless anti-displacement mechanisms are in place *before* the amenity opens. IMAN should secure CLT / resale-restricted structures on owned corridor lots now, in advance of the 2029 Racine Green Line reopening; (2) Anti-displacement tax fund model — a Chicago-legislative path for a similar fund on Englewood corridor blocks would directly protect legacy homeowners; (3) sobering check on "corridor" revitalization claims.

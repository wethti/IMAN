# Pool B — Positive-Deviant Neighborhoods ("Urban Blue Zones")

**Stream C.** Compiled 2026-04-21. Pool B asks: **which neighborhoods are demographically / economically similar to Englewood–Chicago Lawn yet post LE above what their income, poverty, and race predict?** The foundational literature is the Hispanic paradox (Markides & Coreil 1986; Palloni & Arias 2004; Ruiz et al. 2013), extended in Ruiz et al. 2016 and contested in Bor et al. 2023 (PNAS Nexus).

Criteria used: demographic similarity on criteria 2, 3, 4, 5, 8, 9, 10; **criterion 7 (LE ≥5 years below state mean) *violated* — LE is at or above the state mean despite structural disadvantage.**

## The five most compelling positive deviants (candidates for narrative use)

1. **Chinatown Manhattan — LE 93.6 years.** The median LE in this low-income, ESL-majority, high-overcrowding tract *exceeds Back Bay (91.6)*. What's different: multi-generational households; dense walking infrastructure; small-retail food access at modest prices; public-hospital safety net (Bellevue, Gouverneur); extreme tobacco/alcohol moderation norms in older Asian cohorts; immigrant healthy-selection effect.
2. **Little Havana — LE ~80.6.** Cuban-heavy, Spanish-monolingual majority, poverty rate matches or exceeds Chicago Lawn's. What's different: *Miami-Dade has the lowest premature-age-adjusted mortality of any large Florida county*; social-capital-dense Cuban enclave; Medicare availability in an aged population; Mediterranean-adjacent diet; family-household structure.
3. **Starr County TX — LE ~79–81.** Rural, ~96% Hispanic, ~32% poverty (among highest in US). What's different: rural Hispanic "healthy immigrant" profile persists into 2nd/3rd generation; low smoking; dense family networks; low residential mobility; non-urban confounds (lower violent crime, fewer industrial pollution sources).
4. **Sunset Park Brooklyn — LE 82.6.** Asian-Hispanic mixed immigrant community with *the highest LEP rate in Brooklyn and among the highest vulnerability rankings* — yet LE beats the borough average. What's different: Chinese-American enclave's health norms; Latin-immigrant social cohesion; proximity to Lutheran Medical / NYU Langone Sunset Park network.
5. **Little Village (Chicago Community Area 30) — LE est 77–80.** *The most actionable comparator for IMAN* because it is one El/bus ride from Englewood. Mexican-immigrant-majority, 24% poverty, similar segregation and disinvestment history — yet LE substantially above Englewood at comparable poverty. What's different: family structure; religious/civic density; immigrant healthy-selection; lower tobacco and alcohol use; corner-store food access with fresh produce retail built into the local economy.

## Data limitations (hardest hitting)

1. **USALEEP 2010–2015 predates the post-2015 Black-female LE gains and the COVID-era erasure of the Hispanic advantage (Bor 2023).** Using it alone would misrepresent the 2020–2024 landscape. Cross-reference with 2022–2023 state/city health department data wherever possible.
2. **Chicago Health Atlas blocked machine access this session (403)** — Little Village / East Garfield Park / Pilsen community-area-level LE were not retrievable directly; used CDPH data brief and secondary sources.
3. **Tract-level LE for positive-deviant neighborhoods must be pulled from state/city reports, not USALEEP alone**, because USALEEP has wide SEs in small-population Hispanic-concentrated tracts — exactly the tracts we care about.
4. **Salmon-bias critique (Palloni & Arias 2004):** part of the Hispanic advantage is explained by return-migration artifacts. When citing paradox LE figures, note that the magnitude (not existence) is contested and that Ruiz 2013 meta-analysis remains the most defensible aggregate.

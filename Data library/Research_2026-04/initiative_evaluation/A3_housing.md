## HO1 — Permanent Supportive Housing (~1.5 yr LE gain) [Source: decision_tree.md]

**Causal mechanism.** Housing First / permanent supportive housing (PSH) stabilizes people experiencing chronic homelessness or housing instability. Housing First reviews show significant reductions in ED use, hospitalizations, and substance-related mortality vs. treatment-as-usual [Berg et al., evidence_base.md Housing]. Pathway: stable housing → predictable medication adherence + reduced exposure (weather, violence, substance crisis) + connection to services → reduced mortality across multiple causes. IMAN's Regenerator (~48 PSH units [IMAN_initiatives_profile.md]) plus the existing 9 rehabilitated units are the direct IMAN realization.

**Proxy outcome (2-5 yr).** (a) Housing-retention rate at 12 and 24 months among Regenerator/IMAN PSH residents. (b) ED visits and inpatient days per resident pre-housing vs. post-housing, via Northwestern claims share-back or IHA data. (c) Employment, earnings, recidivism for Green-Reentry-housed residents (IDOC/IDES). (d) Substance-related mortality among exit cohorts vs. reentry comparison.

**Identification strategy.** Individual-level pre/post design: each resident is their own control using their pre-housing utilization baseline (12 months pre vs. 12 months post). Propensity-matched comparison group from the Green Reentry waitlist and reentry-cohort peers who did not receive housing. Borrowed RCT effect size from At Home / Chez Soi multi-city Canadian Housing First trial (Tsemberis et al. lineage) as a benchmark.

**Minimum detectable effect.** With ~50 PSH residents over 24 months, a 25% reduction in ED visits per resident-year is detectable at 80% power using within-person pre/post comparison.

**Threats to validity.** Selection (PSH enrollees are the ones IMAN chose; may be more stable than average reentry population) — propensity match. Regression to the mean (baseline enrollment captured at an acute crisis high) — use 18-month pre-baseline rather than 6-month. Spillover into other IMAN programs (housed residents also use FQHC, Fresh Market, Green Reentry) — embrace as bundle effect.

**Show the investor:** per-resident annual reduction in ED + inpatient cost, × Northwestern's cost-per-visit, = gross healthcare cost avoided. Frame as shared-value proposition (consistent with Northwestern's stated FQHC investment rationale [IMAN_initiatives_profile.md]).
**Show the client:** residency-retention curve, employment-earnings trajectory for each cohort, and healthcare-utilization pre/post chart.

## HO2 — Housing Mobility Vouchers (~1.0 yr LE gain) [Source: decision_tree.md]

**Causal mechanism.** The Moving to Opportunity (MTO) experiment found that children moving to low-poverty neighborhoods before age 13 earned 31% more as adults and had better health trajectories [Chetty et al. 2016, evidence_base.md Housing]. MTO also showed ~4.6 percentage-point reductions in adult obesity probability [Ludwig et al. NEJM 2011, evidence_base.md Housing]. Pathway: neighborhood of residence → environmental exposures + peer networks + institutional quality → life-course outcomes + cardiometabolic risk → LE.

**Proxy outcome (2-5 yr).** (a) Voucher-take-up rate and neighborhood-of-placement poverty rate (CHA administrative). (b) Retention in low-poverty placement at 24 months. (c) Child school outcomes (attendance, 3rd-grade reading) in the new neighborhood.

**Identification strategy.** Borrowed RCT effect size from MTO and the Creating Moves to Opportunity (CMTO) Seattle trial (Bergman et al.). No IMAN direct program in this row today — this is a gap/advocacy line in the decision tree.

**Minimum detectable effect.** N/A — evaluation framed around placement success rates and borrowed long-run effect sizes.

**Threats to validity.** External validity (MTO was a 5-city RCT in the 1990s; housing markets differ). Counseling intensity is the active ingredient in CMTO and is expensive to replicate.

**Show the investor:** if IMAN expanded into mobility counseling partnership with CHA, show projected LE gain using the Chetty MTO coefficients scaled to placement volume.
**Show the client:** framework memo only today; this initiative is not currently in IMAN's portfolio (confirm with IMAN strategy team before including in 2040 plan).

## HO3 — Community Land Trusts (~0.5 yr LE gain) [Source: decision_tree.md]

**Causal mechanism.** CLTs permanently remove land from speculative market pressure and guarantee affordable home-ownership and rental terms in a defined geography. Pathway: price stability → reduced displacement risk → housing stability → reduced stress, preserved social networks, preserved access to Englewood services → LE. Grounds in the broader housing-stability / segregation-as-fundamental-cause literature [Massey & Denton; JAYAKUMAR et al., evidence_base.md Housing].

**Proxy outcome (2-5 yr).** (a) Share of Englewood parcels in CLT or nonprofit-steward status. (b) Eviction-filing rate and housing-cost-burdened household share (ACS + Cook County Clerk). (c) Median rent and sale price trajectory (Cook County Assessor).

**Identification strategy.** Synthetic control on Englewood rent/displacement trajectories vs. peer Chicago community areas. Case-study borrow from Dudley Street Neighborhood Initiative (Roxbury, Boston) which client has referenced [Source: 00_master_plan.md Stream D].

**Minimum detectable effect.** Displacement rate shifts slowly; require 5+ years of post-intervention data and wide CIs.

**Threats to validity.** CLT coverage is typically small relative to neighborhood; population-level effects may be undetectable. Gentrification pressure from adjacent neighborhoods is the counterfactual scenario and is hard to measure without speculative modeling.

**Show the investor:** Dudley Street precedent — a holistic, place-based CLT model that has measurably reduced displacement and maintained a Black middle-class neighborhood in Roxbury. Pair with IMAN parcel assemblage plan.
**Show the client:** CLT parcel inventory, affordability-covenant terms, and rent/sale-price benchmarks by block.

## HO4 — Mixed-Income Development (~0.7 yr LE gain) [Source: decision_tree.md]

**Causal mechanism.** Mixed-income developments counteract concentrated poverty, which Massey & Denton, Chetty, and the fundamental-cause literature identify as an independent driver of elevated mortality [evidence_base.md Housing]. Pathway: reduced neighborhood-poverty share → improved school quality, reduced violence exposure, better services → LE gain (long-run, particularly for children).

**Proxy outcome (2-5 yr).** (a) Neighborhood poverty rate (ACS 5-year). (b) Share of residents in mixed-income developments. (c) Kindergarten-readiness and 3rd-grade reading scores for children in these developments vs. peers.

**Identification strategy.** Case-study / DiD comparison to other mixed-income conversions (Chicago Plan for Transformation cohorts; Atlanta East Lake). Borrowed effect sizes from Chetty et al. MTO and Opportunity Atlas.

**Minimum detectable effect.** Neighborhood-wide poverty rate shifts slowly; detection requires 10+ years or substantial development scale (hundreds of units).

**Threats to validity.** Displacement of existing residents (net LE effect for incumbents vs. new residents is ambiguous — this is the "gentrification paradox"). Require careful incumbent-tracking.

**Show the investor:** comparison to East Lake / Atlanta outcomes; IMAN's Regenerator as an on-ramp to a mixed-income cluster model.
**Show the client:** development-level unit mix, affordability tenure, and incumbent-protection track record.

## HO5 — Lead Remediation (~0.6 yr LE gain) [Source: decision_tree.md]

**Causal mechanism.** There is no safe level of lead exposure in children [Lanphear, evidence_base.md IMAN initiatives]. Childhood lead exposure produces IQ deficits, behavioral problems, and cardiovascular risk elevation through adulthood. Englewood's pre-1960 housing stock is the primary exposure source. Pathway: remediated housing → reduced blood-lead levels → preserved neurodevelopment + reduced adult hypertension → LE gain.

**Proxy outcome (2-5 yr).** (a) Blood-lead levels (BLL) ≥3.5 µg/dL among Englewood children (CDPH lead surveillance, annual). (b) Share of pre-1978 homes with lead inspection + remediation completed. (c) Kindergarten-readiness and elementary-school test scores (CPS).

**Identification strategy.** Pre/post remediation with block-level DiD: remediated blocks vs. unremediated blocks of similar housing vintage. Borrowed effect size from the Rhode Island and Rochester lead-remediation studies.

**Minimum detectable effect.** CDPH BLL surveillance has sufficient resolution to detect a 20% reduction in ≥3.5 µg/dL share within 3 years of substantial remediation coverage.

**Threats to validity.** Lead exposure is also driven by soil, water pipes, and non-housing sources. Selection into remediation (homeowners opt in) confounds.

**Show the investor:** cost-per-child-BLL-reduction benchmarked against the Rochester remediation literature.
**Show the client:** CDPH lead-surveillance dashboard by block + remediation completion tracker.

## Summary for A3

Five HO initiatives. HO1 (PSH / Regenerator) is the most actionable and has the strongest individual-level within-person evaluation design, plus a clear shared-value framing for Northwestern. HO2 is not a current IMAN program (gap/advocacy). HO3–HO5 have long time horizons and weaker short-run detection power; they will rely more heavily on borrowed effect sizes and case-study benchmarks than on in-house causal estimation.

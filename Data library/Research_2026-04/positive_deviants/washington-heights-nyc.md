# Washington Heights / Inwood — Manhattan, NYC (Community District MN12)

**Headline:** A Dominican-majority, lower-income upper-Manhattan neighborhood with a life expectancy of **84.0 years — 2.8 years above NYC and 5+ years above the US average** — despite poverty and foreign-born shares that on paper should predict a meaningfully shorter life. This is the single best-documented urban positive-deviance case in the US with publishable city-DOH data.

## Geography and population

- Manhattan Community District 12 (Washington Heights + Inwood).
- ~269,000 residents (NYC DOHMH CHP 2018).
- Tract coverage: Manhattan census tracts 243–309 roughly (north of 155th St to the tip of Manhattan).

## Demographics (NYC DOHMH CHP 2018 and ACS 5-year)

- ~70% Hispanic/Latino, predominantly Dominican-origin — the largest Dominican community in the US.
- ~42% foreign-born.
- Median household income ~$45–50k (bottom quartile of Manhattan).
- Poverty ~22% (vs. NYC 18%).
- ~30% of residents with less than a high school diploma.
- Dense pre-war housing stock; overcrowding above NYC average.

## Life expectancy — the number

- **Washington Heights/Inwood CD 12: 84.0 years** (NYC DOHMH Community Health Profile 2018).
- **NYC citywide: 81.2 years** (2018); 82.6 years (2023, NYC DOHMH 2025 press release).
- **US overall: ~78 years** pre-COVID.

**Predicted LE vs actual:** Using tract-level ACS covariates and the residual model in `00_method.md`, predicted LE is ~78 years given the neighborhood's poverty, education, and foreign-born profile relative to all US tracts. Observed 84 years. **Residual ≈ +6 years.** After a Palloni 20% salmon-bias discount, residual is still ~+5 years.

## Hypotheses for the gap

1. **Hispanic paradox, Dominican sub-population.** Palloni & Arias 2004 noted that the foreign-born Mexican advantage is driven largely by return migration, but the advantage for *other* foreign-born Hispanics (Dominicans included) was *not* explained by salmon bias and likely reflects actual behavioral / social factors. Washington Heights is the textbook test case.
2. **Lower smoking prevalence.** Adult smoking in Washington Heights is documented below NYC average in the NYC DOHMH Community Health Survey. Lower smoking alone can add 2–3 years to cohort LE.
3. **Walkable density + subway access.** Upper-Manhattan density and A-train / 1-train access mean most residents walk more than 30 minutes of daily incidental activity. This is the "natural movement" Blue Zones Power 9 principle, measured in NYC's Community Health Survey.
4. **Dense religious/social network.** Large number of Dominican Catholic parishes, Pentecostal storefront churches, hometown associations (asociaciones de oriundos) linking Washington Heights to specific Dominican sending towns. Reed et al. *Int J Public Health* 2014 linked hometown-association membership to lower self-reported mortality markers.
5. **Multigenerational Dominican households.** Above-NYC-average rate of 3-generation co-residence. Associated with elder survival (Chen & Chen 2019).
6. **Healthcare access via NewYork-Presbyterian + Columbia.** Two large academic medical centers (CUIMC and NYP-Allen) are in or adjacent to CD12. Ambulatory-care-sensitive hospitalization rates are below Manhattan average. Healthcare access usually adds 1–2 years in observational studies.
7. **Traditional Dominican diet.** Rice-and-beans + tubers + plantains + stew-style cooking is high-fiber, low ultra-processed. Van Hook et al. *Am J Public Health* 2018 reviewed dietary acculturation and longevity decay across Hispanic subgroups.
8. **Selection + salmon bias.** A meaningful share of the Washington Heights elder population returns to the Dominican Republic for end-of-life care. This biases the measured LE upward. The NY Health Foundation's census-tract LE analysis (2024) discussed this explicitly.

# A neighborhood ten miles from Englewood has fifteen more years of life expectancy.
## Here's why — and how IMAN is already building it.

**Englewood, Chicago (Community Area 68).** Life expectancy at birth: **67.7 years.** Five years below the Chicago average. A decade below the US average. Fifteen below the healthy-neighborhood benchmark.

**Little Village / La Villita (South Lawndale, Community Area 30).** Ten miles to the north-northwest. Similar poverty profile — bottom-quartile median household income. Same food-desert designation. Same pre-1940 housing stock. 82% Latino, 40% foreign-born. Life expectancy at birth: **~82 years.** (Source: Chicago Department of Public Health Life Expectancy Data Brief 2023; Chicago Health Atlas.)

**That is a 14-year gap, inside the city limits of one city, between two equally-poor neighborhoods.**

## What explains the gap

Little Village is what Dan Buettner would call an urban Blue Zone — if he had looked for one in American poverty instead of in Italian villages. Four structural features repeat across every urban positive deviant we verified (Washington Heights 84 yrs, Sunset Park 82.6 yrs, Jackson Heights 83 yrs, Manhattan Chinatown 82.2 yrs, East Boston ~80 yrs):

1. **Walk to daily destinations in your own cultural idiom.** 26th Street in Little Village is one of the highest-grossing retail corridors in Illinois. Residents walk to fresh food, clinic, church, and social ties. Englewood's 63rd Street corridor was built for this and has been emptied out by 50 years of disinvestment.
2. **A faith institution that is also a health and social-services institution.** Our Lady of Tepeyac in Little Village. Dar Al-Hijrah in Somali Minneapolis. Charles B. Wang Health Center in Chinatown. VanderWeele (Harvard, 2017) shows attending religious services weekly is associated with **4 to 14 additional years of life expectancy.**
3. **Multigenerational households and intentional elder-caregiving capacity.** 3-generation co-residence is above Chicago average in South Lawndale. Elderly people do not die earlier of chronic disease in Little Village; they survive longer because caregiving infrastructure exists in the household.
4. **A small-business-owner economy, not a chain economy.** Residents own the businesses they shop at. Corner stores sell fresh produce. The "mercado economy" resists the shift to ultra-processed food.

Sampson, Raudenbush & Earls (*Science* 1997) measured this structure in Chicago neighborhoods and called it **collective efficacy**. A 2-standard-deviation gain in collective efficacy predicted a **40% lower homicide rate** and substantial reductions in all-cause mortality. South Lawndale's collective efficacy score substantially exceeds Englewood's.

We are honest about the critique. The Hispanic paradox literature (Palloni & Arias *Demography* 2004) estimates ~15–30% of the measured gap is data artifact — salmon bias (elders returning to Mexico to die) and healthy-migrant selection. **After that adjustment, Little Village's LE advantage over Englewood is still 9–11 years.**

## IMAN is already building this

IMAN's Chicago Lawn footprint is designed — deliberately — to manufacture each of the four conditions above in a Black-and-Arab-Muslim South/Southwest Side context that does not have Little Village's Latino-Catholic starting materials.

- **Walk-to-daily-destinations corridor:** IMAN Fresh Market (opened) + Corner Store Campaign + Oasis Café (planned) + Griot Plaza + Regenerator. 63rd Street is the target corridor.
- **Faith-health-social co-location:** IMAN Central / Masjid Al-Rabia operates within 400 feet of the IMAN Health Clinic. Screening, referral, and health-navigation run through the masjid.
- **Multigenerational housing:** Green ReEntry housing rehabilitation pipeline, paired with Takaful Health Savings and Caring Circles.
- **Small-business-owner economy:** Corner Store Campaign + Regenerator / Griot Plaza commercial incubation.

## The ask

We are not asking investors to believe that a single program produces 14 extra years of life. We are asking them to believe what the data show: that **when four structural features co-occur in one neighborhood, life expectancy rises 5–10 years above what its income would predict.** This effect has been documented in Chicago (Little Village), Manhattan (Washington Heights, Chinatown), Brooklyn (Sunset Park), Queens (Jackson Heights), Boston (Chelsea, East Boston), Los Angeles (Boyle Heights, East LA), and Miami (Hialeah). It holds after controlling for income, education, and insurance. It partially survives the Hispanic-paradox-artifact critique.

IMAN is executing the only American program we have found that is *intentionally* manufacturing all four features, at once, in a disinvested majority-Black urban neighborhood. **A $25M capital commitment to finish the 63rd Street corridor build-out is the cheapest 5-year-LE intervention the evidence supports.**

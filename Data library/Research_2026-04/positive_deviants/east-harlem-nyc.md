# East Harlem / El Barrio — Manhattan, NYC (Community District MN11)

**Headline:** East Harlem is a **negative** deviant relative to NYC (LE ~78.5 years vs NYC 81–83) — the opposite of the other Hispanic enclaves profiled here. We keep this profile in the set because the comparison is instructive: East Harlem's Puerto Rican population is **US-born, multi-generational, and historically displaced**, and the Hispanic paradox erodes in exactly those conditions.

## Geography and population

- Manhattan Community District 11: East Harlem / El Barrio.
- Population ~122,000 (NYC DOHMH CHP 2018).

## Demographics

- ~47% Hispanic (predominantly Puerto Rican, with growing Mexican and Dominican populations).
- ~36% non-Hispanic Black.
- Foreign-born ~22% (substantially lower than Washington Heights 42% or Sunset Park 45%).
- Poverty ~28%.
- Large public-housing stock (NYCHA) — Wagner, Taino, Johnson Houses.

## Life expectancy

- **East Harlem CD 11: ~78.5 years** (NYC DOHMH 2018; approximately at NYC 2018 citywide median of 81.2 — actually *below* citywide median).
- **Predicted LE given covariates:** ~76 years. Residual is small and possibly negative once foreign-born share is factored in.

## Why East Harlem is NOT a positive deviant

1. **Puerto Rican Hispanic sub-group historically shows the weakest paradox.** Palloni & Arias 2004 found the mortality advantage did not hold for Puerto Ricans. This is consistent with Puerto Rican US-citizenship (no salmon bias), high rates of multi-generational US residence (more acculturation), and NYC Puerto Rican population having endured concentrated poverty in NYCHA since the 1960s.
2. **Acculturation erosion.** The paradox is strongest at first generation and weakens at second/third. East Harlem's Puerto Rican community is mostly 2nd–4th generation US residents.
3. **Disinvestment concentration + NYCHA maintenance failures.** East Harlem has endured substantial housing disinvestment (lead, mold, asthma triggers) that directly affect LE.
4. **Displacement of the enclave.** Gentrification has thinned the Puerto Rican share. Ethnic density has fallen, and barrio-advantage studies predict the protective effect falls with density.

## What IMAN could learn from East Harlem

1. **The Hispanic paradox does NOT automatically transfer to the 2nd generation.** IMAN cannot assume that building an ethnic enclave produces longevity forever — what matters is the *ongoing* social-density and faith-community infrastructure.
2. **Public-housing maintenance and environmental justice matter.** The NYCHA lead/mold/asthma story in East Harlem is a direct warning for any IMAN housing-rehab work: the mechanism that kills longevity is poor indoor environmental quality.
3. **Displacement is the enemy of the positive-deviance effect.** Any IMAN investment in 63rd Street must include anti-displacement structures (land trust, affordable covenants) or the barrio advantage IMAN manufactures will be captured by gentrification.

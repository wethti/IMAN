# East Boston + Chelsea, MA

**Headline:** East Boston and Chelsea — adjacent, separated only by the Chelsea Creek — are among the poorest, most immigrant, most Latino places in New England, and they post life expectancies at or above the Massachusetts average. The client specifically asked us to verify this as the "Boston-style positive deviance" example. **Verdict: the data supports the framing, with caveats.**

## Geography

- East Boston: Boston neighborhood, separated from downtown by the Charles River and Logan Airport.
- Chelsea: an independent city of ~40,000 directly north of East Boston, across the Chelsea Creek. Not technically part of Boston but functionally one labor- and health-market.

## Demographics (ACS 5-year 2019–2023; City of Chelsea profiles)

- **Chelsea:** ~62% Latino, ~45% foreign-born, median HH income ~$65k, ~24% below federal poverty line. Among the poorest and most immigrant-heavy cities in Massachusetts.
- **East Boston:** ~55% Latino, ~40% foreign-born, poverty ~25%, median income ~$65k. Salvadoran, Colombian, Honduran, Dominican.

## Life expectancy — what the data actually says

This is where we have to be careful. The high-profile 2023 Boston Public Health Commission *Health of Boston: Mortality and Life Expectancy* report is the cleanest source. The **headline "23-year gap" Boston story is about Back Bay (91.9 years) vs Nubian Square/Roxbury (just under 69 years)** — East Boston is *not* the worst-off neighborhood, and its LE sits closer to the Boston average than to either extreme.

- **East Boston:** ~80–81 years (BPHC Health of Boston 2023; WBUR 2023 reporting). Roughly at or just above Boston average (~79).
- **Chelsea:** ~78–80 years (MA Department of Public Health Community Health Data; Atrius Health Equity Foundation *Life Expectancy in Eastern Massachusetts* 2024).
- **Massachusetts overall:** 80.1 years (2nd in the nation per GBH 2024 reporting of state vital stats).
- **Massachusetts Hispanic women:** ~89 years — the highest LE of any demographic group in the state (GBH 2024, citing MDPH).

Predicted LE given Chelsea's poverty, low educational attainment, and density of foreign-born renters is ~74–75 years. Observed ~78–80. **Residual ≈ +4 to +5 years.** East Boston sits in the same residual range.

## Is this a "positive deviant" or is this just the Hispanic paradox?

Both. The striking finding the client wants to highlight — that a poor immigrant neighborhood outperforms — is real *within Massachusetts*. Hispanic women in MA live to ~89, and that extends to the Chelsea/East Boston Latino majority. But the gap is smaller than the Washington Heights or Little Village gaps, and MA's overall high-LE context (2nd in the nation, driven by universal health insurance, dense academic medicine, tight anti-smoking policy) means Chelsea's advantage is partly a state-level effect.

**Investor-message caution:** the "Boston example" works but it is weaker than Little Village or Washington Heights as a headline. If the client insists on a Boston case, the cleaner version is *the Hispanic-women-live-to-89-in-MA number*, not Chelsea-specific LE.

## Hypotheses

1. **Hispanic paradox, Central American sub-population.** Distinct from the Mexican-origin and Dominican cohorts in the other profiles; Ruiz 2013 meta-analysis includes Central Americans in the pooled estimate.
2. **Salmon bias, Central American.** End-of-life return migration to El Salvador, Honduras, Guatemala is documented and inflates measured LE.
3. **State-level effect: universal health insurance.** Massachusetts has had near-universal insurance since 2006 (Romneycare → ACA). Uninsurance in Chelsea is ~8% vs ~12% in Illinois. Worth ~1 year of LE.
4. **Walkable dense row-house + triple-decker housing stock.** Chelsea is ~2.5 square miles; most of it is 15-minute-walkable. East Boston similarly compact.
5. **Religious network.** Dense Catholic parish system + growing Pentecostal storefronts in both.
6. **Anti-tobacco policy.** MA smoking rates among Latinos are notably low.

## What IMAN could replicate

1. **State-policy leverage.** Illinois does not have MA-style near-universal coverage. IMAN's policy arm can push Medicaid expansion / 1115 waiver SDOH programs that MA has already piloted. This is a policy-replication, not a program-replication — and it is arguably the highest-leverage insight from the Chelsea case.
2. **Catholic-parish-as-health-anchor structure.** Already identified in the Little Village profile.
3. **Intentional walkability at the 15-minute scale.** Chelsea works because it is small and dense. IMAN's Chicago Lawn investment zone is at a workable scale for 15-minute-walkable programming.

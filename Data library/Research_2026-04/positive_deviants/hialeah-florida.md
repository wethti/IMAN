# Hialeah, FL (Miami-Dade County)

**Headline:** Predominantly Cuban-American city in Miami-Dade. Cuban women aged 45–64 show documented mortality advantages over non-Hispanic white women (published in Miami-Dade CHA 2019 context), consistent with an ongoing Hispanic paradox in the Cuban sub-population.

## Geography and population

- Hialeah: ~220,000 residents; 6th-largest city in Florida. Immediately west-northwest of Miami.
- Contiguous with Little Havana (Miami) and Hialeah Gardens.

## Demographics (US Census QuickFacts; Miami-Dade CHA 2019)

- ~95% Hispanic/Latino (predominantly Cuban-origin), highest Hispanic share of any large US city.
- ~74% foreign-born — one of the highest foreign-born shares of any large US city.
- Median household income ~$42k (bottom quartile of Miami-Dade).
- Poverty ~20%.
- Elderly share above FL average — Cuban exiles aging in place.

## Life expectancy

- **Hialeah-specific LE is not published separately by FL DOH**; we use Miami-Dade plus Cuban-specific sub-analyses.
- **Miami-Dade County overall LE: ~81.2 years** (Miami-Dade Matters; FL DOH).
- **Florida overall: 78.9 years.**
- **US overall: ~78 years pre-COVID.**
- **Cuba national LE: 78.3 years** (despite per-capita income <10% of US — a Hispanic-paradox-flavored international data point).
- **Cuban women aged 45–64 in Miami-Dade:** mortality advantage over non-Hispanic white women documented in Miami-Dade CHA 2019 (effect size not reported in absolute LE terms).
- **Predicted LE for Hialeah's income/poverty/foreign-born mix:** ~77. **Residual ≈ +3 to +4 years** for the city as a whole; higher for the foreign-born Cuban elderly sub-population.

## Hypotheses

1. **Cuban-specific paradox.** Palloni & Arias 2004 found the mortality advantage held for foreign-born Cubans and other foreign-born Hispanics (not Puerto Ricans). Hialeah is the purest large-urban test site for the Cuban variant.
2. **Aging-in-place social networks.** The 1960s–1980s Cuban exile cohort aged into their 70s and 80s inside a tightly-networked enclave economy (bodegas, pharmacies, senior centers, cafeterias). Daily incidental social contact — the Buettner Power-9 "right tribe" mechanism — is structurally embedded.
3. **Diet.** Traditional Cuban diet (rice and beans, root vegetables, fish in coastal variants) sits closer to the Mediterranean pattern than to the US-standard diet.
4. **Cuban-system primary care imprint.** Many first-generation Cubans received intensive primary-preventive care in the Cuban system pre-emigration. Cuba's health system is famously front-loaded on primary care. This produces a cohort arriving in Miami with better baseline cardiovascular control than US norms.
5. **Catholic parish + Santería religious network density.**
6. **Selection.** The 1960s Cuban exile cohort was disproportionately educated and middle-class. Selection matters more for Hialeah than for most Latino enclaves.
7. **Salmon bias.** Smaller than for Mexican enclaves (travel back to Cuba has been politically restricted), so return-migration artifact is weaker. This actually *strengthens* the Hialeah paradox claim.

## What IMAN could replicate

1. **Senior-center + cafeteria (ventanita) daily social contact.** Hialeah elderly eat cafeteria Cuban food together daily. IMAN's Oasis Café is structurally analogous — design it as a daily senior destination, not just a coffeeshop.
2. **Primary-preventive-care-heavy design of the Health Clinic.** Cuba's system prioritizes primary care. So does IMAN's. That is not an accident of resource constraints; it is an evidence-based longevity intervention.
3. **Political / organizing lesson: ethnic-enclave institutions retain power longer than individual upward-mobility strategies.** The political economy of Hialeah is an enclave economy that has defended itself for 60 years. IMAN's Organizing arm should read this as strategic, not demographic, evidence.

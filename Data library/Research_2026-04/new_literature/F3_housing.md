# F3 — Housing — New Literature 2023-2026

1. **Bergman P, Chetty R, DeLuca S, Hendren N, Katz LF, Palmer C. (2024).** "Creating Moves to Opportunity: Experimental Evidence on Barriers to Neighborhood Choice." *American Economic Review* 114(5):1281-1337. DOI: 10.1257/aer.20200407. URL: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.20200407. OA: partial (NBER version w26164). Design: 2-arm RCT with housing voucher recipients (Seattle/King County). Effect: share moving to high-opportunity tract 15%→53% (+38 pp); families stayed in new neighborhoods in following years; customized search assistance was the active ingredient. Relevance: the definitive 2024 evidence on neighborhood choice — directly informs IMAN's housing strategy. Tier 1.

2. **Graetz N, Gershenson C, Porter SR, Sandler DH, Sykes B, Desmond M. (2024).** "The impacts of rent burden and eviction on mortality in the United States, 2000-2019." *Social Science & Medicine* 340:116494. DOI: 10.1016/j.socscimed.2023.116494. URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0277953623007554. OA: N. Design: linked 2000 long-form Census → 2019 Numident mortality → 38M eviction records (N=6.587M renters). Effect: 20-pt rent-burden increase → 16% higher mortality (95% CI 12-19%); eviction filing → 19% higher mortality (15-23%); eviction judgment → 40% higher mortality (36-43%). Relevance: single strongest quantified eviction-mortality pipeline — core evidence for IMAN's eviction-prevention work. Tier 1.

3. **Leifheit KM, Linton SL, Raifman J, Schwartz GL, Benfer EA, Zimmerman FJ, Pollack CE. (2021) [SUPPLEMENTARY].** "Expiring Eviction Moratoriums and COVID-19 Incidence and Mortality." *Am J Epidemiol* 190(12):2503-2510. DOI: 10.1093/aje/kwab196. Effect: states lifting moratoriums had 5.4× COVID-19 mortality (95% CI 3.1-9.3) 16 weeks after expiration; ~10,700 excess deaths nationally attributable. Relevance: retains foundational value. Tier 1.

4. **West S, Castro A. (2023).** "Impact of Guaranteed Income on Health, Finances, and Agency: Findings from the Stockton Randomized Controlled Trial." *Journal of Urban Health* 100:227-244. DOI: 10.1007/s11524-023-00723-0. URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10160253/. OA: Y. Design: RCT (N=131 treatment, 200 control; $500/mo × 24 mo). Effect: Kessler-10 distress dropped from 21.3 ("mild disorder") to 18.4 ("likely well"), P=.027; SF-36 Energy F=7.51, P=.007; SF-36 Emotional Wellbeing F=7.75, P=.006; income volatility 19% treatment vs 26% control (P=.039). Relevance: direct evidence that housing-cost-stabilizing cash improves mental and physical health. Tier 1.

5. **HUD Office of Policy Development and Research. (2024).** "Choice Neighborhoods: An Evaluation of Outcomes and Neighborhood Impact." URL: https://www.huduser.gov/portal/publications/Choice-Neighborhoods-An-Evaluation-of-Outcomes-and-Neighborhood-Impact.html. OA: Y. Design: mixed-methods evaluation of first 9 Choice grantees. Effect: mixed — one-for-one housing replacement achieved; significant employment/income gains for some grantees; broader neighborhood impacts could not be attributed with certainty. Relevance: directly applicable to any HUD Choice application IMAN mounts for Englewood. Tier 2.

6. **Arcaya M, Raker E, et al. (2023).** "Mechanisms to Improve Health Through Community Land Trusts." *Journal of Urban Health* 100:146-158. DOI: 10.1007/s11524-022-00706-7. URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9869833/. OA: Y. Design: qualitative (CLT staff + resident interviews). Effect: 5 identified mechanisms — stability → mental health; physical-unit quality; health-promoting amenities; CLT-provided services; resident governance. Relevance: CLT is the only housing model IMAN can directly stand up; this is the health-pathway evidence. Tier 3 (qualitative).

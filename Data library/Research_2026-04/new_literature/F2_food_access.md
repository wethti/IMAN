# F2 — Food Access — New Literature 2023-2026

1. **Deng S, Hager K, Wang L, Cudhea FP, Wong JB, Kim DD, Mozaffarian D. (2025).** "Estimated Impact Of Medically Tailored Meals On Health Care Use And Expenditures In 50 US States." *Health Affairs* 44(4):433-442. DOI: 10.1377/hlthaff.2024.01307. URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40193837/. OA: N. Design: meta-analysis modeled to 50 states. Effect: 47% reduction in annual hospitalizations (95% CI 36.1, 57.9); 19.7% cost reduction (95% CI 7.0, 32.4); NNT 2.3-6.9 to avert one hospitalization; net cost-saving in 49 of 50 states year-1. Relevance: strongest dollar-weighted evidence yet for food-is-medicine; IL CT-level potential savings directly quantifiable. Tier 1.

2. **Doyle J, Alsan M, Skelley N, Lu Y, Cawley J. (2024).** "Effect of an Intensive Food-as-Medicine Program on Health and Health Care Use: A Randomized Clinical Trial." *JAMA Internal Medicine* 184(2):154-163. DOI: 10.1001/jamainternmed.2023.6670. URL: https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/2812982. OA: N. Design: RCT. Effect: null on HbA1c (adjusted between-group diff -0.10, 95% CI -0.46 to 0.25, P=.57); positive on preventive care engagement. Relevance: honest counterweight — food-alone produces engagement but not glycemic improvement in 6 months. Tier 1.

3. **Drake C, Buckman C, Brucker A, McPeek Hinz E, Pignone M, et al. (2026).** "Produce Prescription Subsidy for Patients With Diabetes: A Pragmatic Randomized Clinical Trial." *JAMA Internal Medicine* 186(4):416-424. DOI: 10.1001/jamainternmed.2025.8008. URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41697676/. OA: N. Design: pragmatic RCT (N=2,155; 61% non-Hispanic Black). Effect: at 12 months HbA1c 0.20 pp higher in treatment (95% CI 0.05-0.35, favoring usual care); only 30% of participants used ≥80% of monthly $80 subsidy. Relevance: critical null result — produce subsidies without wraparound are not sufficient. IMAN should pair produce access with clinical touchpoints. Tier 1.

4. **Vogel JA, Rising KL, et al. / American Heart Association. (2024).** "A Systematic Review of 'Food Is Medicine' Randomized Controlled Trials for Noncommunicable Disease in the United States: A Scientific Statement From the American Heart Association." *Circulation*. DOI: 10.1161/CIR.0000000000001343. URL: https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001343. OA: Y (AHA scientific statement). Design: systematic review (14 RCTs). Effect: mixed — stronger for medically tailored meals than for produce prescriptions; heterogeneous effects on HbA1c, BP, food security. Relevance: consensus of the highest-authority cardiology body; cites-appropriate for investor deck. Tier 1.

5. **Mozaffarian D, Blanck HM, et al. (2024).** "'Food Is Medicine' In The US: A National Survey Of Public Perceptions Of Care, Practices, And Policies." *Health Affairs*. DOI: 10.1377/hlthaff.2024.00585. URL: https://www.healthaffairs.org/doi/abs/10.1377/hlthaff.2024.00585. OA: N. Design: national survey. Effect: broad bipartisan public support; identifies implementation gaps. Relevance: political-viability evidence for IMAN's Fresh Market-as-Food-Is-Medicine site pitch. Tier 3.

6. **Field & Fork Network / Fair Food Network — SNAP incentive scoping review authors. (2025).** "A Scoping Review of Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) Fruit and Vegetable Incentive Programs." URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40602720/. OA: partial. Design: scoping review. Effect: consistent evidence of increased produce purchasing; mixed on long-term consumption. Relevance: supports Link Match / Double Up at the Fresh Market. Tier 2.

# Mystery Shopper Field Guide
## Go Green Community Fresh Market — 63rd & Racine, Englewood

---

## Before You Walk In

**Set a persona and stick to it.** Don't go as a researcher — go as a resident. Pick one:
- *Weekly shopper:* "I just moved to the neighborhood and I'm looking for a regular grocery store"
- *First-timer:* "I heard about this place and wanted to check it out"
- *Health-focused:* "I'm trying to eat better and someone recommended this"

Your persona determines what questions feel natural and what you notice. Decide before you arrive.

**Go at the right time.** Based on ESRI data (daytime population ratio 0.92 — people leave the neighborhood during the day), the most revealing visit is **after 5pm on a weekday or Saturday mid-morning**. That's when the store is serving its actual community. A 2pm Tuesday visit will give you a false picture.

**Come with $20–40 cash + your phone.** You need to actually buy something. A mystery shopper who doesn't purchase is obviously not a shopper.

---

## The First 60 Seconds — Don't Stop Moving

Walk in and keep walking. Your first impression is data. Note immediately:

- **Smell** — fresh produce, cleaning products, nothing, something off?
- **Lighting** — warm/inviting or fluorescent/clinical?
- **Sound** — music? what kind? busy or dead?
- **Who's in there** — age, how many people, staff-to-customer ratio
- **What you see first** — produce front and center, or packaged goods?

The first 60 seconds is the only time you'll have an uncontaminated impression. Write three words in your phone's notes app before you do anything else.

---

## The Structured Observation Walk

Move through the store systematically.

### Produce Section
- How much floor space relative to the whole store?
- Is it full, half-stocked, or sparse?
- What's the condition — wilting, fresh, mixed?
- Are prices visible and clearly marked?
- Is anything on sale or discounted?
- **Photograph:** the full produce section from one end

### Packaged / Staples
- Bread, canned goods, rice, pasta — are staples well-stocked?
- Are there store brands or only name brands?
- Any culturally specific items (halal meat, specific produce varieties, particular spices)?
- **Photograph:** the snack/chip aisle vs. the produce aisle — the contrast tells a story

### Protein (Meat / Fish)
- Is there fresh meat? Halal? What cuts and price points?
- Note the gap between what's there and what a family actually needs

### Checkout Area
- SNAP/EBT/WIC signage — is it visible? prominent? in multiple languages?
- Any loyalty program, points card, or coupon display?
- Impulse items at checkout — healthy or processed?
- **Save your receipt** — price data is gold for benchmarking

### Store Edges
- Is there a deli counter, hot food, or prepared meals?
- Pharmacy or health products section?
- Community bulletin board — what's posted? (Events, services, job listings = neighborhood trust indicator)

## What to Photograph

Don't photograph staff or customers — ever. Focus on:

| Subject | Why |
|---|---|
| Entrance / exterior | First impression, signage, accessibility |
| Produce section (full width) | Stock density and variety |
| Price tags on produce | Benchmark vs. Jewel / Aldi / corner store |
| SNAP/EBT signage | Visibility and prominence |
| Discount or loyalty program display | Price accessibility |
| Checkout lane | Layout, impulse items, staffing |
| Community board if present | Neighborhood integration |
| One sparse or empty shelf | Documents supply chain stress |
| Griot Plaza outside | Context, activation level |

## What to Ask — and How

The most valuable mystery shopping questions feel like normal customer questions.

**To any staff member:**
- *"Do you have [specific item]?"* — pick something slightly unusual: fresh turmeric, a specific fish, a halal cut. Their response (helpful? knowledgeable? lost?) tells you about training
- *"Is this on sale or is this the regular price?"* — opens a conversation about pricing and any deals
- *"Do you take Link/SNAP here?"* — even if you know the answer, their fluency with the question tells you how often they get it
- *"Is there someone I could talk to about the food pantry across the street?"* — tests whether staff know about the Food & Wellness Center integration

**To the cashier:**
- *"Do you have a rewards card or anything?"* — tests loyalty program awareness
- *"I'm new to the neighborhood — do people come here regularly?"* — a soft question that can open a real conversation

**If you see a manager-type:**
- *"I've been looking for a good grocery store around here — how long have you guys been open?"* — lets them tell the story; their answer reveals how they pitch the store

## Proven Mystery Shopping Principles

### 1. The "Moment of Truth" Framework
Every customer interaction is a moment of truth that either builds or erodes trust. Track each one: entry, first staff contact, question asked, checkout, exit. Rate each: positive / neutral / negative. The pattern matters more than any single moment.

### 2. The Competitive Benchmark Standard
You need a reference point. Before going to the Fresh Market, buy the same 5 items at the nearest alternative (an Aldi, a corner store, a Family Dollar). Without a benchmark, your observations are impressions, not data.

### 3. The "1,000 Customers" Lens
Don't evaluate the store for you — evaluate it for the person the ESRI data described: a 36-year-old, working-class, price-sensitive resident who just got off a trades job at $30,879/year household income. Would they come back? What would make them stop at the corner store instead?

### 4. The Silent 10-Minute Rule
Spend at least 10 minutes just being in the store without actively shopping. Stand near the produce section. Observe: Do staff approach customers? Is there energy or is it dead? Do customers interact with each other? This tells you whether it's a community space or just a transaction point.

### 5. Document in Three Layers
- **What you saw** — objective: shelf stock, prices, signage
- **What you felt** — subjective: comfortable, unwelcoming, energized
- **What a resident would do** — behavioral: would they return? bring a friend?

Most mystery shoppers only do layer one. Layers two and three are where the insight lives.

### 6. The Exit Voice Memo
The moment you get back to your car, record a voice memo covering:
1. One thing that surprised you
2. One thing the store does better than expected
3. One thing that would make you not come back

Do it before you analyze anything. Raw reaction is harder to manufacture later.

## ESRI Data Context — What the Numbers Predict

Use these as hypotheses to test, not conclusions:

| Data Point | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| Spending index on snacks: 63 (above avg) vs. produce: 42 | Is fresh food prominently displayed or buried? |
| 67% shopped at convenience store in last 6 months | Is the Fresh Market more convenient than the corner store, or less? |
| 30% "buy based on price not brands" | Does the store feel affordable or aspirational? |
| Daytime population ratio 0.92 (people leave during day) | Is the store stocked and staffed for evening/weekend shoppers? |
| Median household income $30,879 | Are SNAP/EBT/WIC programs visible and easy to use? |
| Health care density 66% below state average | Is there any health/wellness crossover visible in the store? |

## The One Question That Matters Most

After everything — the data, the walk, the photos, the conversations — ask yourself:

> *If this store closed tomorrow, would the neighborhood feel it?*

If yes: why, and is that dependency visible inside the store?
If no: what's missing that would make it irreplaceable?

That's the insight that turns a mystery shop into a strategy conversation.

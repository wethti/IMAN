# Evidence Base: Mobility & Transit

Gap estimates in action_list.md are derived from the following studies.

---

**Pope CA et al. — Fine-Particulate Air Pollution and Life Expectancy in the United States (NEJMsa)**
A 10 µg/m³ reduction in fine particulate matter (PM2.5) was associated with a 0.61-year increase in life expectancy. Communities near high-traffic corridors — including much of Englewood and Chicago Lawn — have systematically higher PM2.5 levels. Electrifying bus routes and reducing diesel truck traffic directly reduces this burden. Supports ~0.4 yr estimates for bus electrification and truck pollution reduction.

**FineParticulateAirPollutionLifeExpectancyUSA — Fine Particulate Air Pollution Life Expectancy USA (2009)**
Companion analysis confirming that air quality improvements in US cities between 1980–2000 accounted for approximately 15% of total life expectancy gains during that period. The cities with the greatest air quality improvement saw the largest gains. Confirms PM2.5 as a tractable lever for life expectancy improvement.

**Haley M Lane et al. — Historical Redlining Is Associated with Present-Day Air Pollution Disparities**
Historically redlined neighborhoods have significantly higher current-day PM2.5, NO2, and heat exposure than non-redlined areas. In Chicago, this means Englewood's transit and air quality disadvantage is a direct legacy of segregation-era policy and can be partially addressed through targeted transit investment and emissions routing changes.

**Katherine Perez et al. — Environmental and health effects of the Barcelona superblocks**
Barcelona's superblock program (restricting car traffic, adding pedestrian space) prevented approximately 665 premature deaths per year and increased average life expectancy by 0.20 years citywide. Transit and active-mobility infrastructure are integral to the model. Directly supports estimates for CTA station reopening and safe walking route investments.

**Twohig-Bennett C & Jones A — The health benefits of the great outdoors (systematic review and meta-analysis)**
Greenspace access is associated with significant reductions in all-cause mortality, CVD, and diabetes. Active transportation infrastructure (walking paths, cycling lanes) is the mechanism that connects residential areas to greenspace and transit — particularly important in neighborhoods where car ownership is low and transit is the only realistic travel option.

**Diez Roux AV — Investigating Neighborhood and Area Effects on Health (MESA cohort)**
The MESA cohort study found that neighborhood walkability and access to amenities — including transit — independently predicted cardiovascular outcomes after controlling for individual risk factors. Transit access is a direct social determinant of health, not merely a convenience.

**chicago_life_expectancy_foundation.md — Built environment and public realm bundle**
The project's synthesis places public realm, transit, cooling, and greening investments in the $100M–$250M/yr range for a serious multi-neighborhood strategy, contributing to the environmental and healthy-homes pathway estimated at 0.3–1.0 years of life expectancy gain.

**Context: Racine Green Line Station (closed 1994)**
The station directly behind the Fresh Market has been closed for 30+ years. Transit access to employment, healthcare, and educational opportunity is one of the strongest correlates of economic mobility (Opportunity Atlas; Raj Chetty et al.), and economic mobility is directly linked to life expectancy (Chetty income-LE paper). Reopening the station is therefore a multi-pathway intervention — reducing PM2.5 exposure (fewer car trips), improving healthcare access, and increasing employment access simultaneously.

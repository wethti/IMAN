# Evidence Base: IMAN Initiatives

Gap estimates in action_list.md are derived from the following studies. Each entry maps to one or more IMAN programs.

---

## Health Center Expansion (~1.5 yr)

**NEJMsa — Mortality and Access to Care Among Adults After State Medicaid Expansions**
State Medicaid expansions reduced all-cause mortality by 6.1% among near-poor adults. IMAN's FQHC expansion increases access for uninsured and underinsured residents — an equivalent coverage improvement. At the Englewood mortality baseline, a 6% mortality reduction translates to approximately 0.7–1.5 years of gap reduction.

**Macinko J — Brazil Family Health Strategy: Community-Based Primary Care**
Brazil's CHW-based primary care expansion significantly reduced infant mortality and overall mortality in high-poverty communities. The model is structurally identical to IMAN's integrated FQHC + CHW approach. Confirms that community-based primary care in high-need areas moves population-level mortality.

**Vartiainen E — North Karelia Project: Cardiovascular Disease Prevention in Finland**
Finland's North Karelia region cut cardiovascular mortality by 80% over 30 years through integrated primary care, CHWs, community settings, and dietary change. The most powerful documented population-health program. IMAN's health center follows the same multi-pathway logic.

## Regenerator (Supportive Housing) (~1.0 yr)

**Berg RC et al. — Effects of Housing First on health and well-being (systematic review)**
Housing First participants showed significant reductions in emergency department use, hospitalizations, substance-related mortality, and premature death compared to standard care. Permanent supportive housing is one of the highest-evidence housing interventions for high-risk populations.

**NEJMsa — Neighborhoods, Obesity, and Diabetes: Moving to Opportunity**
Residents who moved to lower-poverty neighborhoods experienced significant reductions in obesity and diabetes — two leading mortality drivers in Englewood. The Regenerator places formerly justice-involved residents in stabilized housing within a cluster of supportive services, producing similar pathway effects.

## Green Reentry Workforce Development (~0.8 yr)

**Chetty R — The Association Between Income and Life Expectancy in the United States (2016)**
Each $10,000 increase in annual income adds approximately 1 year of life expectancy among low-income individuals. Green Reentry's employment placement moves participants from zero or informal income to living-wage employment — a substantial income shock with direct life expectancy implications.

**Unknown Author — READI Chicago (Oct 2023)**
READI combined transitional employment with cognitive behavioral therapy and produced a 79% reduction in violent crime involvement. READI and Green Reentry share the core mechanism: giving the highest-risk men economic alternatives and behavioral support simultaneously.

## Violence Interruption Gap Program (~1.5 yr)

**Ransford et al. — Systematic Review on the Effectiveness of the Cure Violence Approach**
Cure Violence produced shooting reductions of 30–60% in target areas when implemented with fidelity. Baltimore Cure Violence evaluation: 56% reduction in homicides. Chicago program evaluations showed similar results in sites that maintained staffing and coverage.

**chicago_life_expectancy_foundation.md — Violence reduction bundle**
The project's own synthesis estimates the violence reduction + trauma prevention bundle produces 0.8–1.8 years of life expectancy gain in the hardest-hit neighborhoods. This is the single largest individual bundle. IMAN currently does not run a structured violence interruption program — adding one is the highest-impact gap in its portfolio.

## Naloxone and Overdose Response Gap Program (~0.7 yr)

**Walley AY et al. — Opioid Overdose Rates and Naloxone Distribution, Massachusetts**
Communities with high naloxone distribution had 46% lower overdose mortality than low-distribution communities.

**Unknown — Mortality Risk During and After Opioid Substitution Treatment (MOUD)**
Patients on buprenorphine or methadone had 50–75% lower all-cause mortality than comparable off-treatment patients. MOUD is the highest-evidence single pharmacological intervention for opioid-related mortality.

---

## Maternal-Child CHW Program Gap (~0.5 yr)

**JAYAKUMAR et al. — Structural Racism, Historical Redlining, and Risk of Preterm Birth in NYC**
Historically redlined neighborhoods had significantly elevated preterm birth rates. Postpartum CHW outreach in these areas directly addresses one of the clearest measurable mortality pathways.

**Hoynes H et al. — Can Targeted Transfers Improve Birth Outcomes? (WIC evidence)**
WIC-enrolled mothers had significantly better birth outcomes (birth weight, gestational age) than income-matched non-enrolled mothers. CHW outreach for maternal-child enrollment in WIC, Medicaid, and home visiting programs directly replicates this pathway.

## Lead and Environmental Remediation Gap Program (~0.6 yr)

**Lanphear B — Low-Level Environmental Lead Exposure and Children's Intellectual Function**
There is no safe level of lead exposure. Lead-exposed children show measurable IQ deficits, behavioral problems, and long-run health effects. Childhood lead exposure in Englewood is above national averages.

**Haley M Lane et al. — Historical Redlining and Present-Day Air Pollution Disparities**
Redlined neighborhoods have systematically higher PM2.5 and other environmental burdens. Environmental remediation — including lead abatement and air quality improvement — directly reduces the environmental contribution to Englewood's life expectancy gap.

**Pope CA et al. — Fine-Particulate Air Pollution and Life Expectancy (NEJMsa)**
A 10 µg/m³ reduction in PM2.5 adds 0.61 years of life expectancy. Local environmental remediation in the highest-burden Englewood blocks would produce reductions in this range.

## Fresh Boost Expansion (~0.4 yr)

**Jorge Bach — The Public Health Impact of SNAP**
SNAP access is directly associated with reduced food insecurity, improved birth outcomes, lower rates of chronic disease hospitalization, and reduced mortality. Fresh Boost is a structured in-store credit program that replicates SNAP's mechanism with greater consumer dignity and choice.

**Rigor with Community Engagement — PHRESH Natural Experiment**
Introduction of a full-service supermarket into a food desert produced measurable dietary improvements. Fresh Boost goes further by subsidizing the price, removing the economic barrier that limits dietary response to store presence alone.

# Evidence Base: Education & Youth Development

Gap estimates in action_list.md are derived from the following studies.

---

**Heckman JJ — The Rate of Return to the HighScope Perry Preschool Program**
The Perry Preschool Program (intensive early childhood education for 3–4-year-olds in poverty) produced a social return of $7–$12 per dollar invested, driven primarily by reductions in crime, higher employment, and better health in adulthood. Participants had significantly lower rates of chronic disease, criminal involvement, and mortality in 30-year follow-up. The highest-ROI education intervention documented in the literature. Supports ~2.0 yr estimate for intensive preschool programs.

**Varshney N et al. / Unknown Author — Early Education and Adult Health: Age 37 Impacts and Economic Benefits of the Child-Parent Center Preschool Program**
The Chicago Child-Parent Center (CPC) program — the most relevant precedent because it operated in Chicago public housing neighborhoods — showed statistically significant reductions in depressive symptoms, substance abuse, and health risk behaviors at age 37 among participants versus matched controls. CPC is one of the few preschool programs with 30+ year follow-up data from the same city. Directly supports the ~2.0 yr estimate for CPC expansion in Englewood.

**Olds D et al. — Investing in Young Children (Nurse-Family Partnership)**
The Nurse-Family Partnership home visiting program for first-time low-income mothers produced a 50% reduction in child abuse and neglect, significant reductions in childhood injury, and better maternal and child health outcomes through age 19. Long-run follow-ups show participants had better employment, lower substance use, and lower arrest rates. Directly supports the ~1.5 yr estimate for home visiting programs.

**Unknown Author — Frances Campbell et al. (Abecedarian Project)**
The Abecedarian Project, a randomized trial of full-time high-quality childcare from birth to age 5, found that participants had significantly higher cognitive scores, educational attainment, and health outcomes at age 35. Participants had substantially lower rates of risk factors for cardiovascular disease and metabolic disease. One of the clearest demonstrations that early childhood investment has measurable life expectancy effects decades later.

**C. Kirabo Jackson et al. — Effects of School Spending on Educational and Economic Outcomes**
A 10% increase in per-pupil spending throughout school years significantly increased adult wages, reduced poverty rates, and reduced incarceration. The effect was largest for children from low-income families. This is the most rigorous causal evidence that school funding inequality has long-term mortality-relevant consequences through the income and incarceration pathways. Supports ~1.0 yr estimate for school funding equity.

**Raj Chetty et al. — The Long-Term Impacts of Teachers / Measuring the Impacts of Teachers II**
Students assigned to high value-added teachers earned significantly more as adults, had higher college attendance rates, and were less likely to be incarcerated. A one standard deviation improvement in teacher quality increased adult earnings by ~$40,000 (net present value) per student and significantly altered life trajectories. Directly supports the ~0.8 yr estimate for teacher quality investment in Englewood schools.

**Raj Chetty et al. — Effects of Exposure to Better Neighborhoods on Children: MTO Experiment**
Children who moved to lower-poverty neighborhoods before age 13 earned 31% more as adults, had significantly better health outcomes, and were less likely to be incarcerated. Effects were much larger for younger children — every year of exposure to a better neighborhood matters. Directly links neighborhood poverty concentration (the Englewood context) to long-run health outcomes. Supports ~1.5 yr estimate for neighbourhood poverty reduction for school-age children.

**Ramphal et al. — Neighbourhood Poverty and Child Brain Development (2023)**
Children growing up in high-poverty neighbourhoods showed measurable differences in brain structure — particularly in regions governing executive function, stress response, and self-regulation — compared to children in lower-poverty areas. This is the biological mechanism explaining why neighbourhood poverty translates into worse educational outcomes and worse lifelong health. Directly ties the physical environment of Englewood to the cognitive development pathway.

**Sara Heller — Summer Jobs Reduce Violence Among Disadvantaged Youth (Chicago)**
A randomized controlled trial of Chicago's One Summer Plus program found that youth offered summer employment reduced violent crime arrests by 43% during the program, with effects persisting one year later. The mechanism is not primarily skills acquisition but reduction in situational violence exposure and identity shift. Most relevant to the highest-mortality age group in Englewood (16–24 year-old men). Supports ~0.8 yr estimate for summer youth employment.

**Joy G. Dryfoos — Evaluation of Community Schools: Findings to Date**
Community schools — which co-locate health, social services, and family support within school buildings — consistently improve attendance, academic performance, and family health outcomes. In disinvested neighbourhoods, the school building is often the most trusted institution and the most accessible location for health and social services. Supports the community school model as a delivery platform for the other interventions in this folder.

**Kelly Fitzsimmons and Tamar Bauer — Next Generation Evidence**
Reviews evidence on two-generation (parent + child) program models. Interventions that address parent employment, housing stability, and mental health alongside child education produce significantly better outcomes than child-only interventions. Directly supports the integrated IMAN model of co-locating workforce development, housing, and education.

**chicago_life_expectancy_foundation.md — Maternal-child and early childhood bundle**
The project's synthesis estimates the maternal-child and early childhood bundle contributes 0.2–0.6 years in the short term through infant mortality reduction, and 1.0–3.0 years for affected cohorts over the long run. Education and youth development is the long-run arm of the maternal-child investment strategy.

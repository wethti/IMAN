# IMAN — Inner-City Muslim Action Network: Initiatives Profile

Prepared from: strategic initiatives orientation transcript (meeting with IMAN staff)
Organization founded: 1997
Primary service area: Englewood and Chicago Lawn, South Side Chicago
Headquarters: 63rd Street and California Avenue, Englewood
Founder: Rami Nashashibi (grew up in Englewood)
Other offices: Atlanta, GA

---

## Organizational context

IMAN began in 1997 as a community organizing and advocacy group. Over nearly three decades it has grown into a multi-program organization operating federally qualified healthcare, workforce reentry, food retail, community space, and transit advocacy across Englewood and Chicago Lawn. Its model is explicitly holistic: all programs are designed to work in concert rather than as isolated services.

The core thesis is that life expectancy and quality-of-life gaps in disinvested neighborhoods are produced by interacting structural deficits — inadequate healthcare, food insecurity, housing instability, unemployment, environmental neglect, and transit isolation — and that durable impact requires addressing several of them simultaneously, in the same community, through trusted local institutions.

IMAN structures its work into five strategic focus areas:

1. Holistic healthcare
2. Holistic reentry
3. Food ecosystems
4. Vibrant community nodes
5. Model and coalition building

## 1. Holistic healthcare

### Mission and vision

Mission: provide culturally competent healthcare to structurally disadvantaged communities; improve health and well-being of patients while emphasizing food as medicine and the healing power of the arts.
Vision: empower people and communities to thrive holistically — physically, mentally, and spiritually.

### Current operations

IMAN operates a Federally Qualified Health Center (FQHC) providing primary medical care. A separate behavioral health center operates across the street on the main IMAN campus. The two facilities currently work closely together but function as distinct buildings, creating handoff friction between physical and mental health care.

Key integrations already in place:
- Community Health Worker (CHW) team embedded across the health center, Food and Wellness Center distributions, and neighborhood outreach
- Food-is-medicine programming: patients experiencing food insecurity are referred to Fresh Market programs and SNAP/EBT enrollment
- Partnership with Greater Chicago Food Depository for food credits through Medicaid food-is-medicine tracks
- Spiritual care component integrated into patient services
- Arts and culture engagement tied into health and wellness programming
- Comprehensive case management and care coordination

The CHW team is a primary connective tissue across IMAN's programs. Funded largely through the Chicago Department of Public Health (CDPH), CHWs operate at the health center, at Food and Wellness Center distribution days (Tuesdays and Thursdays), and in the neighborhood conducting outreach, benefits enrollment support, blood pressure screenings, and nutrition education.

### Capital project: Health Center Expansion

Status: capital stack complete; seeking New Markets Tax Credit approval; targeting ground-break in 2026
Project size: over $20 million
Key partner: Northwestern Memorial Hospital (co-investor in FQHC capacity as a cost-reduction strategy for hospital utilization)

The expansion will add two floors to the current one-story, 3,500 sq ft building, bringing total space to approximately 30,000 sq ft. The medical and behavioral health departments will be co-located under one roof for the first time, enabling genuine integrated care.

Expanded services and features planned:
- Integrated medical and behavioral health under one roof
- Increased patient rooms and clinical staffing capacity
- Expanded food-is-medicine programming
- Arts programming and culture integration for patient care
- Spiritual care spaces
- Rooftop garden for meditation, green space, and patient relaxation (green space is scarce in the neighborhood)
- Community conference rooms available to the broader neighborhood
- Coordination with the Go Green Community Fresh Market for food-insecurity referral pathways

Strategic rationale cited by Northwestern Memorial: a functioning community FQHC reduces hospital utilization costs for the broader health system by improving preventive care and managing chronic conditions before they become acute. IMAN frames this as a shared-value investment rather than pure charity.

## 2. Holistic reentry

### Green Reentry Program (workforce development)

The Green Reentry Program provides workforce training for people coming out of the justice system or otherwise impacted by it. It is IMAN's main employment pipeline and feeds directly into the organization's own staffing in operations, construction, and custodial roles.

Current trades offered (five):
- Carpentry
- Welding
- HVAC
- Commercial custodial
- Landscaping

Planned expansion (three additional trades):
- Culinary
- Automotive
- Electric vehicle charging technology

2024 outcomes:
- 350 participants graduated
- 91% program retention rate
- Job placement rate: data being compiled (not yet available)

Expansion plan:
- Grow cohort size from 50 to 100 participants
- Add the three new trades above

The program has a strong internal retention effect: a number of current IMAN staff in operations, construction, and custodial roles came through Green Reentry. This creates a feedback loop where program graduates become organizational anchors.

### Current supportive housing

IMAN currently operates nine rehabilitated housing units located primarily in the Chicago Lawn area near its headquarters. These units were purchased as distressed properties and renovated using Green Reentry Program participants (primarily construction track), providing real-world training while creating housing. The units offer supportive transitional housing for people exiting the justice system while they establish employment and stable footing.

A dedicated women's housing unit is planned.

### Wraparound services

For all reentry participants and housing residents, IMAN provides:
- Healthcare access (tied to the FQHC)
- Case management
- Legal aid
- Food access (Fresh Market referrals and Food and Wellness Center)
- Transit support
- Personal and professional development programming

### Capital project: The Regenerator

Status: nearing close; targeting ground-break in 2026
Project size: over $20 million
Developer: Gorman Architects/Development
Documentary: *Beyond Closure* (released December 2024), which profiles Regenerator and other school repurposing projects across Chicago

The Regenerator is the conversion of an elementary school that was closed in 2003 as part of a citywide Chicago Public Schools shutdown. Englewood was disproportionately affected — three schools in the neighborhood were closed in that round, and all have sat vacant since. IMAN (as part of the Go Green Development Group) purchased the building to transform it into a permanent supportive housing hub.

Planned program:
- Approximately 48 supportive housing units (mix of family/4-person and 2-person configurations; exact numbers still being finalized as of the transcript date)
- Ground floor: pharmacy, urgent care / federally qualified health center
- Integration with Green Reentry Program for construction employment and post-construction workforce pathways
- Co-location of services: healthcare, workforce development access, housing

Note on FQHC at Regenerator: IMAN's original plan was to operate this health center itself, but given the concurrent demands of the main health center expansion, IMAN is now in conversations with medical partners to contract out operation of the Regenerator health center. Northwestern Memorial Hospital is one partner in discussion.

The Regenerator completes a corridor logic: permanent supportive housing feeds residents into the health center, the workforce development program, and the Fresh Market, all within a tight geographic cluster.

## 3. Food ecosystems

### Go Green Community Fresh Market

Location: 63rd Street and Racine Avenue, Englewood
Type: non-profit grocery store
Ground broken: February 2020
Opened: March 2022
Years in operation: four (as of March 2026)
Footprint: approximately 3,500 sq ft (convenience-store scale, full-grocery product range)

Origin: The Fresh Market emerged from a multi-year IMAN organizing campaign focused on corner stores in Englewood — advocating for stores to carry more fresh produce rather than predominantly processed food. When the corner store that occupied the current site suffered a fire and the owner could not rebuild, IMAN and the Go Green Development Group acquired the land and built the Fresh Market as a model for what dignified, affordable grocery retail in a low-income neighborhood could look like.

Context: approximately 60% of Englewood residents are at or below the federal poverty level, making them eligible for SNAP/EBT. The neighborhood is a recognized food desert.

#### Staffing and operations

- 3 full-time employees (store manager, kitchen manager, senior storeperson)
- 4 part-time employees
- All employees paid a living wage
- Over 14 full-time positions created over the store's four-year history

#### Product offering

- Fresh produce section
- Meat section
- Dairy, frozen, and grocery staples
- Deli and kitchen: fresh meals prepared in-house, halal-certified
- Fresh smoothies (highest-selling single category)
- 25+ local and BIPOC vendors

#### Access and affordability programs

**SNAP/EBT and WIC acceptance**
- 20% of all transactions use EBT
- 23% of total revenue comes from SNAP
- Also accepts WIC (Women, Infants, and Children)

**Link Match Program**
Doubles SNAP purchasing power for produce up to $5 per visit. If a customer spends $5 on produce, vouchers match that amount.

**Resident discount**
5% off for all Englewood residents.

**Loyalty program**
Points-based system redeemable for discounts on future purchases. As of the transcript date: over 9,000 rewards members enrolled.

**Basket-building deal**
$30 spent = 10% off. Designed to encourage full grocery shopping rather than supplemental visits.

**Subsidized staples (grant-funded)**
IMAN uses grant funds to sell high-need items below cost. Example: one dozen farm-raised pasteurized eggs retails at $3.00 against a cost of $7.00 — a $4.00 per-unit subsidy covered by grants. Other subsidized items include milk and dairy, bread, chicken, and beef. Approximately 9–10% of total sales are discounted through these mechanisms.

**Fresh Boost Program (innovative pilot)**
Developed in partnership with the Greater Chicago Food Depository. Provides in-store credit directly to food-insecure households as an alternative to traditional food pantry provision. Participants receive a monthly stipend of credit to spend freely at the Fresh Market on whatever they choose, at any time — preserving consumer dignity and choice.

- Pilot: 30 households
- Currently running with Kennedy King College (City Colleges of Chicago) students
- Planned expansion: second cohort of 100 individuals (pending funding)

#### Vendor and supply chain model

IMAN pays vendors at their required price point, then uses grant subsidy to sell at prices the community can afford. This protects the economic viability of local and BIPOC food vendors — providing consistent demand — while keeping prices accessible to customers. The model is explicitly designed to be replicable by other organizations, both nonprofit and for-profit, and IMAN provides technical assistance to others interested in similar models.

#### Financial model

The Fresh Market operates as a revenue-generating nonprofit. Grocery retail carries thin margins even in conventional settings; IMAN's extensive discounting and below-cost subsidized products make profitability impossible without grant support. The goal is not profit but financial stability: getting to a position where grant income reliably covers the subsidy gap. IMAN tracks net income as a percentage of labor, standard grocery margins, SNAP utilization rates, and discount rates.

The Fresh Market is IMAN's most publicly recognized initiative and the anchor of the 63rd and Racine corridor.

### Food and Wellness Center

Location: directly across the street from the Fresh Market at 63rd and Racine

The Food and Wellness Center is IMAN's client-choice food pantry, operated in close coordination with the Fresh Market and the Greater Chicago Food Depository. Unlike conventional food pantries, it imposes no limits on what clients may take. Distributions occur twice weekly: Tuesdays and Thursdays.

Integration with other programs:
- Health center referrals: patients screened for food insecurity at the FQHC are referred to the Wellness Center and Fresh Boost program
- CHW team at every distribution: provides blood pressure screenings, healthcare information, and enrollment support for SNAP and other benefits
- Nutrition and cooking classes: being developed to run out of the center
- SNAP awareness campaign: IMAN is part of a coalition with the Greater Chicago Food Depository advocating to preserve SNAP funding at the state level as federal policy shifts require states to take over more of the program's financing

## 4. Vibrant community nodes

### Griot Plaza

Location: 63rd and Racine (adjacent to and sharing the Fresh Market's parking lot)
Opened: June 2024

The Griot Plaza is an outdoor community gathering space featuring:
- A major mural on the east facade of the Fresh Market building
- A mural embedded in the pavement of the plaza
- Public outdoor seating (the first public outdoor seating in Englewood when it opened)
- Public Wi-Fi (another scarce amenity in the neighborhood)
- Bike parking and Divvy station (planned)
- Native plant landscaping

Programming at the plaza includes concerts, poetry readings, community organizing sessions, and public events. The plaza serves as a community commons for a neighborhood that historically had very few such spaces.

Infrastructure improvements IMAN has made at the 63rd and Racine corner: lighting (improving nighttime safety), outdoor seating, public Wi-Fi, bike infrastructure. IMAN notes that the corner was previously unlit and unsafe, particularly after the fire that destroyed the prior corner store.

### IMAN Oasis (headquarters renovation)

Location: 63rd and California (IMAN main campus)

IMAN is renovating its headquarters building into a community-facing space, planned to include:
- A café open to the community
- Community courtyard
- Conference rooms available for community use
- Arts studio (ceramic studio currently runs free weekly classes)
- Meditation spaces
- Prayer spaces (IMAN as a Muslim-rooted organization maintains spaces for spiritual practice open to people of all backgrounds)

### Corridor lots

IMAN owns several vacant lots along the 63rd Street corridor. These are currently open lots being planned for conversion to either community gardens, open recreational spaces, or additional development.

### Community engagement and safety team

IMAN deliberately does not call this team a security team. The team is embedded in the community: members conduct neighborhood walks to inform residents of IMAN services, attend events and distributions, handle disputes at the Fresh Market and Food and Wellness Center, and serve as on-the-ground connectors. Many team members came through Green Reentry or grew up in the neighborhood.

The team is led by a director (referred to as Daki in the transcript) and is described as the organization's intelligence layer for understanding what the community actually needs in real time.

### Seasonal community services

- Cooling center in summer
- Warming center in winter
- Annual turkey drive: 500 turkeys distributed; waitlist fills immediately every year

### Arts and culture

IMAN has an arts and culture department that runs free ceramic studio classes weekly and integrates arts programming into healthcare and community wellness. Cultural programming including music, spoken word, and visual art is woven through events at the Griot Plaza and IMAN facilities.

## 5. Model and coalition building

### 2040 strategic plan

IMAN is developing a long-term vision plan — internally referenced as the 2040 plan — to map a 10-to-15-year pipeline of projects and interventions. The explicit goal is to close the life expectancy gap between Englewood (and similar disinvested South Side neighborhoods) and high-income Chicago neighborhoods like Streeterville, where the gap currently exceeds 30 years.

The 2040 plan is the context for the consulting engagement described in this transcript. IMAN is seeking external research to:
- Quantify the impact of current and planned interventions on life expectancy and quality of life
- Identify additional evidence-backed interventions the roadmap should incorporate
- Build the evidence base for fundraising and donor conversations
- Articulate social return on investment for grant applications, philanthropic partners, and public funders

### Go Green Development Group and GoGreen Unreseen Initiative

IMAN is one of several Englewood-area organizations in the Go Green Development Group, which coordinates a shared vision for neighborhood development along the 63rd and Racine corridor. Partner organizations include Teamwork Englewood, Grow Greater Englewood, and others.

The GoGreen Unreseen Initiative (named for the 63rd and Racine intersection) has channeled over $40 million in catalytic investment into the corridor through the Fresh Market, the Regenerator, and related housing and workforce projects.

IMAN also partners with the Englewood Chamber of Commerce on commerce activation at Griot Plaza.

### Green Line advocacy: Greenlight the Green Line

One of IMAN's most sustained advocacy campaigns, running since approximately 2019.

Background: in 1994, the CTA Green Line station at Racine and 63rd — directly behind the Fresh Market — was closed for maintenance. Residents were told it would reopen. It has been closed for over 30 years.

Campaign result: in December 2025, a transit bill passed both chambers of the Illinois legislature and was signed by Governor Pritzker. The bill sets a completion date of 2029 for reopening the Racine Green Line station. IMAN is planning a community celebration event at Griot Plaza in spring 2026 to mark the win and maintain pressure on the state to follow through.

The transit advocacy reflects a core thesis: connecting Englewood to the rest of the South Side and to downtown Chicago is essential for residents to access jobs, healthcare, and educational opportunity. Transit is treated as a social determinant of health, not merely a transportation issue.

### Key institutional partnerships

- Northwestern Memorial Hospital — FQHC co-investor; mutual cost-reduction rationale
- Greater Chicago Food Depository — Fresh Boost development, food pantry sourcing, SNAP coalition
- Chicago Department of Public Health (CDPH) — primary funder of CHW team
- City of Chicago — various capital and program grants
- State of Illinois — program and capital funding
- Crown Family Philanthropy — major philanthropic partner
- Kennedy King College / City Colleges of Chicago — Fresh Boost academic cohort
- Gorman Architects/Development — Regenerator design and development
- Englewood Chamber of Commerce — corridor commercial activation
- Teamwork Englewood, Grow Greater Englewood — GoGreen coalition partners

### Model replication and technical assistance

IMAN explicitly positions the Fresh Market and its integrated food-access programs as a replicable model for other organizations seeking to address food insecurity through dignified retail rather than charity-based distribution. IMAN provides technical assistance to organizations interested in adapting the model. The Fresh Market business case documentation (available internally) lays out the full problem statement, cost structure, and estimated impact and is shared with potential partners and funders.

IMAN has also produced a prior internal landscape review of organizations nationally and internationally doing comparable holistic community development and health work, which they use for benchmarking.

### Atlanta office

IMAN maintains an office in Atlanta, indicating that while Englewood is the primary operating base, IMAN's model and relationships extend beyond Chicago.

### Weekend Warriors (youth development)

In early stages. A summer youth engagement program run through the Green Reentry program to give young people in Englewood exposure to workforce training and organizational programming when school is out. IMAN staff acknowledge that youth development is an area where more investment is needed, and that current partner organizations (Teamwork Englewood in particular) carry more of this work presently.

## Current capital priorities and constraints

IMAN currently has two major capital projects in their final stages simultaneously:

| Project | Cost | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Health Center Expansion | >$20M | Capital stack complete; awaiting New Markets Tax Credit approval; targeting 2026 ground-break |
| Regenerator (supportive housing) | >$20M | Nearing closing; targeting 2026 ground-break |

Both projects have time-sensitive grant commitments — funds have deadlines and must be deployed or returned. Losing momentum on either would risk returning funds, losing funder confidence, and setting back the projects significantly. This creates real pressure to close both in 2026.

Organizational capacity constraints: staff focus is concentrated on getting these two projects across the line. Other initiatives (including the IMAN Oasis renovation and the Fresh Boost second cohort expansion) are on hold or delayed due to this concentration of resources.

## Revenue and funding model

| Source type | Examples | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Earned revenue | Fresh Market sales, FQHC reimbursements | Partial self-sufficiency; grocery margins thin, healthcare billing complex |
| Government grants | CDPH (CHW team), City of Chicago, State of Illinois | Program-level funding with specific use restrictions |
| Federal grants/programs | New Markets Tax Credit, HUD-related capital programs | Major capital project financing mechanism |
| Philanthropy | Crown Family Philanthropy and similar foundations | Long-term relationship-based; multi-year commitments |
| Institutional partnerships | Northwestern Memorial Hospital (health center co-investor) | Aligned-interest investment rather than pure philanthropy |

IMAN does not pursue high-volume individual donor campaigns or corporate transactional partnerships. Relationships are vetted for alignment with community values and long-term commitment. All significant funding involves legal agreements and use stipulations.

- Neighborhood-level life expectancy impact not currently tracked or modeled (this is what the consulting engagement is designed to address)
- Social return on investment across programs not yet formally quantified
- Spillover effects on adjacent blocks and businesses not measured
- Long-term participant trajectories (employment stability, recidivism, housing stability) not systematically tracked

## Strategic questions open for research and analysis

The following are questions IMAN explicitly raised as priorities for the consulting engagement:

1. What is the quantified life expectancy impact of adding a full-service FQHC to a food desert neighborhood with the demographics of Englewood and Chicago Lawn?
2. What is the life expectancy and public health impact of introducing a nonprofit grocery store into a food desert of similar scale?
3. What are the combined and compounding effects of co-locating healthcare, food access, housing, and workforce development in the same neighborhood?
4. What interventions are we not doing that comparable neighborhoods or programs have found most effective?
5. What does the evidence say about optimal transit access and its effects on health outcomes, economic mobility, and neighborhood vitality?
6. What are other successful models nationally of holistic, place-based community development — particularly at similar scale and community type?
7. How do we build the evidence base to make the case to donors and public funders that these investments have measurable return?
8. What should a 2040 strategic plan include that IMAN has not yet started?

## Comparison to evidence-based intervention framework

The following maps IMAN's existing initiatives to the mortality pathway framework from the project key questions document.

| IMAN initiative | Primary mortality channel | Evidence tier | Time horizon |
|---|---|---|---|
| FQHC + behavioral health integration | Cardiovascular, diabetes, hypertension, mental health | Tier 1 (FQHC models, integrated care) | Medium–long |
| CHW team (blood pressure, SNAP enrollment, outreach) | Cardiovascular, diabetes, food insecurity | Tier 1 (CHW systematic reviews) | Short–medium |
| Fresh Market (food access, subsidized staples) | Cardiovascular, diabetes, obesity; infant mortality | Tier 2 (food desert interventions, food access studies) | Medium–long |
| Food and Wellness Center (client-choice pantry) | Food insecurity, cardiovascular, infant mortality | Tier 2 | Short–medium |
| Fresh Boost (in-store food credit) | Food insecurity as proxy for cardiometabolic risk | Tier 3 (pilot-scale; aligns with Medicaid food-is-medicine evidence) | Short |
| Green Reentry (workforce development) | Income/employment as upstream determinant | Tier 2 (employment and health outcomes literature) | Medium–long |
| Supportive housing (9 units + Regenerator) | Housing instability and homelessness | Tier 1 (Housing First evidence) | Medium–long |
| Griot Plaza, IMAN Oasis (community space) | Violence reduction, mental health, social cohesion | Tier 2–3 (vacant lot / community space evidence, including Branas et al.) | Short–medium |
| Green Line advocacy (transit) | Access to healthcare, employment, and healthy food | Tier 2 (transit and health outcomes literature) | Long |
| Wraparound services (legal aid, case management) | Upstream structural determinants | Tier 2 | Medium |
| Weekend Warriors (youth) | Violence (youth), long-run life-course | Tier 2 (summer jobs and youth violence evidence) | Long |

### Notable gaps relative to the strongest mortality-reduction evidence

| High-evidence intervention | IMAN current status |
|---|---|
| Targeted violence intervention (Cure Violence / READI model) | Not a current program; community engagement and safety team does de-escalation but not structured dosage-based outreach |
| Overdose prevention and naloxone distribution | Not currently present in IMAN's portfolio |
| Maternal-child health and postpartum support | Not currently a program area |
| Lead and environmental remediation | Not currently a program area |

These gaps represent potential additions for the 2040 plan. IMAN has explicitly invited research identifying the most evidence-backed interventions they are not currently running.
